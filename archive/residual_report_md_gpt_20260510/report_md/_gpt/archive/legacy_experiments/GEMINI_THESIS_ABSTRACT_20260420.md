# Thesis Abstract — Hardware-Instance Overfitting and Robust Deployment in Organic Optoelectronic CIM

This dissertation began with a disquieting observation: a vision model trained on one organic optoelectronic array could score eighty-eight percent, yet collapse to ten percent the moment it met a fresh hardware instance. That gap became the central puzzle. Standard hardware-aware training fails on fresh instances because the optimizer does not learn robust representations; it memorizes the specific noise fingerprint of the training-time device, a phenomenon this thesis identifies and characterizes as hardware-instance overfitting. The remedy is Ensemble HAT, which treats each training forward pass as a Monte Carlo draw and marginalizes over instance variability rather than encoding it. Decomposed along axes of cadence and noise profile, the approach reveals a systematic taxonomy in which deterministic single-instance training collapses to ten percent, while ensemble resampling restores eighty-six percent with bounded variance. Beneath these headlines lies a deeper structure. This thesis constructs a failure-mode atlas spanning representational collapse, attention-path fragility, severe nonlinearity bottlenecks, correlated device-to-device mismatch, and retention drift, and translates that atlas into a deployment envelope that tells engineers when an analog chip is ready and when it is not. Simulation can bound the risk, but only measured-device calibration can close it. The three core contributions are a reusable simulation framework, the discovery of hardware-instance overfitting as an independent phenomenon, and a data-driven deployment envelope for organic edge inference. Together, they replace single-array benchmarking with distributional reasoning and lay the groundwork for a decade of analog CIM systems that are trained for the fleet, not the device.
