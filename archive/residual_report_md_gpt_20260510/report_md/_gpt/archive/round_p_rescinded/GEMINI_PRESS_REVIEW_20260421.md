# Press Release Pedagogical Review

**Source:** `KIMI_PRESS_RELEASE_V2_20260421.md`
**Reviewer:** Gemini Subagent
**Date:** 2026-04-18

---

### 1. Jargon Density
**Score: 2/5**
Terms like "nonlinearity," "fresh-instance," and "Ensemble HAT" appear repeatedly, yet "nonlinearity" is never defined in plain language and "HAT" is never spelled out.
**Fix:** Add a one-sentence plain-language gloss for each technical term on first use (e.g., "nonlinearity — the degree to which electrical response deviates from a straight-line relationship").

### 2. Number Overload
**Score: 2/5**
The release piles on percentages (86%, 88.53%, 10.00%, 30%, 32.60%) and error bars (±0.08%, ±1.54%) without prioritizing which figures matter most to a lay reader.
**Fix:** Lead with a single anchor number per section (e.g., "roughly 86% accuracy recovered") and move precise statistics to a parenthetical or footnote.

### 3. The Dual Message
**Score: 3/5**
The "Fix" and "Limit" sections create structural clarity, but the distinction between "moderate" and "severe" nonlinearity is never visually mapped for the reader, risking confusion about when each result applies.
**Fix:** Insert a short transition sentence or sub-head that explicitly frames the two regimes ("moderate distortion" vs. "severe distortion") before presenting each result.

### 4. Headline Clarity
**Score: 4/5**
The headline communicates both the upside and downside in accessible language, though "organic computer chips" may read as biological rather than carbon-based electronics to some lay readers.
**Fix:** Consider a brief qualifier such as "organic (carbon-based) computer chips" or leave as-is if the institutional style guide allows.

### 5. Quote Authenticity
**Score: 3/5**
The first quote is punchy and credible, but the second quote drops jargon ("moderate-NL side," "severe-NL side," "process windows") that sounds like it came from a paper rather than a human speaking to a reporter.
**Fix:** Rewrite the second quote in conversational language (e.g., "On the moderate side, this removes a psychological barrier... On the severe side, it tells fabricators which process windows to avoid").

### 6. Call to Action
**Score: 2/5**
The release explains what the findings mean for engineers and fabricators, but it never tells a general reader why they should care or what they should do next.
**Fix:** Add one sentence in the closing paragraph explaining the reader takeaway (e.g., "For the public, this means cheaper, greener AI chips are closer to reality — but only if manufacturers stay within a specific quality window").

---

## Final Verdict

**Needs simplification — see items above.**

The press release is structurally sound and the headline works, but jargon density, number overload, and a weak call to action will likely lose non-specialist readers before they reach the bottom of the page. A light edit targeting the six items above should make it ready for the institutional comms team.
