## What Would Falsify the Structural Hypothesis?

### The Structural Hypothesis

We claim that under severe write nonlinearity (NL $\geq$ 2.0), the attention pathway---comprising the query-key-value (QKV) transformation and the output projection---in a first-order behavioral surrogate introduces a fundamental generalization barrier that no training-recipe modification can overcome. Specifically, the barrier arises because a first-order Taylor approximation of the non-ideal write operation is insufficient to capture the higher-order curvature introduced by the attention mechanism's multiplicative interactions. In this view, the fresh-instance failure observed at NL $\geq$ 2.0 is not an artifact of insufficient training data, suboptimal hyperparameters, or architectural under-parameterization; rather, it is a structural consequence of the interaction between a low-fidelity nonlinearity model and the attention block's inherent functional form. This hypothesis predicts a hard ceiling: even with infinite training data, arbitrary optimization budget, and perfect regularization, the first-order surrogate will never generalize to fresh instances when NL $\geq$ 2.0.

### Falsification Experiment 1: QKV-Only Linearization (CX-J1b)

The first and most targeted falsification experiment is **CX-J1b**, in which only the QKV transformations are linearized while the MLP blocks and the attention output projection remain at NL = 2.0. If linearizing QKV alone is sufficient to break the ceiling and restore generalization to fresh instances, then the structural hypothesis is wrong in its present form: the barrier would not be a property of the "attention pathway" broadly construed, but would instead implicate the QKV sub-block specifically. This would imply that the projection layer, despite also residing within the attention pathway, does not participate in the structural failure mechanism, and our hypothesis would need to be narrowed to QKV-specific nonlinearity interactions. The distinction matters because it shifts the engineering focus from redesigning the entire attention pathway to modifying only the QKV transformation, a materially easier intervention.

### Falsification Experiment 2: Full-Attention Linearization (CX-J1c)

The second falsification experiment, **CX-J1c**, extends CX-J1b by linearizing the full attention pathway---both QKV and the output projection---while retaining NL = 2.0 in all MLP layers. If this experiment breaks the ceiling, the structural hypothesis is falsified because we explicitly claim that even full attention linearization is insufficient to eliminate the barrier when the MLP blocks remain nonlinear. Our hypothesis asserts that the failure is not localized to any single sub-component of the attention block but emerges from the structural coupling between a first-order nonlinearity surrogate and the attention mechanism as a functional unit. Should CX-J1c succeed, it would demonstrate that the barrier is either (a) not structural at all, or (b) structural but located elsewhere---for example, in the MLP blocks or in the interaction between attention and MLP nonlinearities. Either outcome would require a substantial revision of our theoretical framework.

### Falsification Experiment 3: Higher-Order Surrogate (CX-J1d)

The third and most decisive falsification experiment is **CX-J1d**, which replaces the first-order Taylor surrogate of the write nonlinearity with a second- or third-order Taylor expansion while keeping all other architectural and training choices fixed at NL $\geq$ 2.0. If a higher-order surrogate breaks the ceiling, the structural hypothesis is falsified because the barrier would be shown to be surrogate-fidelity specific rather than an unavoidable structural property of the attention pathway. This outcome would imply that the failure mechanism is one of approximation error---a quantitative deficiency in the nonlinearity model---rather than a qualitative structural incompatibility. Such a result would be scientifically valuable: it would redirect the research program from architectural interventions (linearizing attention) toward higher-fidelity nonlinearity modeling, with immediate implications for the design of compact behavioral surrogates in analog and neuromorphic hardware emulators.

### Prediction

Our prediction is that **none** of these three experiments will break the ceiling. We expect that QKV-only linearization (CX-J1b) will fail because the projection layer remains nonlinear and continues to introduce unmodeled curvature; we expect that full-attention linearization (CX-J1c) will fail because the structural barrier persists in the interaction between nonlinear MLPs and the linearized attention pathway; and we expect that higher-order surrogates (CX-J1d) will fail because the barrier is not merely a matter of Taylor truncation but reflects a deeper structural limitation of separable, block-wise nonlinearity approximations in multiplicative architectures. If this prediction holds across all three experiments, the structural hypothesis is strongly supported---not proven, but rendered the most parsimonious explanation consistent with the evidence. Conversely, if any single experiment breaks the ceiling, we gain an equally valuable positive result: a direct engineering pathway toward overcoming fresh-instance failure and, simultaneously, a precise constraint on the scope and mechanism of the structural barrier. In either case, the experimental program is designed to produce decisive, interpretable outcomes, consistent with the Popperian principle that a hypothesis earns its scientific status by exposing itself to genuine risk of refutation.
