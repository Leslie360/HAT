# 1. Introduction

Compute-in-memory (CIM) and processing-in-memory (PIM) architectures are widely motivated by the energy cost of moving data between memory and arithmetic units in edge inference systems \citep{horowitz2014computing,peng2020dnnneurosim}. For organic optoelectronic synaptic devices, however, the central question is more specific than generic AI acceleration. Device papers can report multilevel conductance states, retention windows, optical sensitivity, and low static power, but those measurements rarely answer the deployment question that system designers actually face: are the measured behaviors already sufficient for modern edge-vision models, or will the stack fail once mapped onto a realistic hybrid analog/digital inference path?

Organic optoelectronic and electrochemical synaptic devices are compelling because they offer multilevel conductance tuning, optical sensitivity, and ultra-low static power \citep{xu2025emerging,guo2024organic}. Yet most device-facing studies still stop at small or idealized network validation. Variability, converter precision, retention drift, instance transfer, and deployment-time calibration are often omitted or treated qualitatively \citep{zeng2023organicmemristor,jung2024organicfilaments}. This leaves a practical decision gap between the quantities that materials researchers can measure early in a device program and the system-level questions that must be answered before a hardware stack can be judged viable.

That gap is especially visible at the simulator level. The best-known variability-aware organic precursor is the Monte Carlo study of Alibart *et al.*, which evaluated a simple organic-memristive perceptron under repeated trials \citep{alibart2016physical}. General CIM simulators such as DNN+NeuroSim and MemTorch have subsequently matured for inorganic resistive memories \citep{peng2020dnnneurosim,lammie2022memtorch}, but they are not organized around the organic-device questions that matter most here: profile substitution from partial characterization, optical-front-end effects, and deployment decisions for hybrid CNN/Transformer inference. In other words, existing tools are useful, but they do not by themselves close the gap from partial organic-device characterization to deployment-facing risk assessment.

This paper addresses that gap through a profile-driven, hardware-aware simulation framework centered on organic optoelectronic synaptic transistors for edge-vision inference. The framework starts from crossbar-aware mapping rules, injects quantization, cycle-to-cycle (C2C) and device-to-device (D2D) variability, ADC distortion, retention drift, nonlinear write surrogates, and physical-front-end effects directly into the forward path, and keeps the surrounding training and evaluation code path fixed while device assumptions change through a shared profile interface. We evaluate the framework across ResNet-18, ConvNeXt-Tiny, and Tiny-ViT-5M. This keeps both convolutional and hybrid transformer deployment regimes in scope.

The paper is organized around four deployment-facing questions. First, is nominal conductance quantization really the dominant bottleneck once scale recovery and hardware-aware training are enabled? Second, what fails first as tasks become harder: quantization, converters, instance shift, or richer device physics? Third, is same-instance robustness enough, or does deployment collapse on fresh hardware realizations? Fourth, which physical stress regimes can be retrained around, and which ones remain hard boundaries for the current recipe? We treat the framework as a first-order behavioral simulator rather than a routed-chip emulator. IR drop, sneak paths, temperature fluctuations, differential-pair layout asymmetry, optical crosstalk, and pulse-faithful programming physics are therefore flagged as out-of-scope physical effects whose implications are revisited explicitly in Section 6. Our results show that quantization alone is often not the main limit in the canonical regime, while converter precision, complexity scaling, fresh-instance transfer, and richer physical stress dominate the practical risk. The strongest positive result is that Ensemble HAT raises fresh-instance Tiny-ViT accuracy from 10.00% to 86.37 ± 1.54%, while a literature-derived Zhang et al. (2025) OPECT profile transfers to 88.53% through the same interface. The strongest negative result is that severe nonlinear write remains a hard failure mode at 27.72 ± 0.82%, even after retraining.

The main contributions are therefore fourfold. First, we provide a PyTorch-based materials-to-system simulation workflow for organic optoelectronic CIM under joint quantization, variability, retention, and peripheral constraints. Second, we extend organic-device robustness analysis beyond small perceptrons to contemporary vision backbones, including a hybrid transformer deployment. Third, we integrate multiple non-idealities in one stack, including nonlinear write surrogates, proportional state-dependent noise stress tests, and profile substitution. Fourth, we show that the same framework can ingest a literature-derived device profile and produce a deployment-facing answer without requiring code-path changes. We note that the present framework operates at the simulation level and does not include array-level non-idealities such as IR drop, sneak-path currents, or temperature dependence; these limitations and their expected impact are discussed in Section 6.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work on organic neuromorphic devices, CIM simulation frameworks, and hardware-aware training. Section 3 presents the hybrid mapping strategy, analog crossbar model, variability and retention formulations, and the energy-estimation methodology. Section 4 summarizes the experimental setup, including the model suite, datasets, and experiment matrix. Section 5 reports the cross-dataset results, retention behavior, front-end compensation results, and physically extended stress tests. Section 6 discusses the dominant bottlenecks, architectural differences, and materials-facing implications. Section 7 concludes with the scope and limits of the present first-order behavioral framework.
