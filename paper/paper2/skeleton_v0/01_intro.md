# §1 Introduction

Analog compute-in-memory (CIM) architectures eliminate the data-movement bottleneck that constrains conventional digital inference by executing dense matrix–vector multiplications directly inside memory arrays. For edge vision workloads, where energy budgets are measured in milliwatts and latency in milliseconds, this paradigm offers a plausible path to orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains. Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged as the architecture of choice for many visual tasks, delivering competitive accuracy with global receptive fields and parameter-efficient scaling. Yet ViTs are also among the most demanding models to deploy on analog substrates: their self-attention mechanisms rely on high-fidelity query-key-value (QKV) transformations and delicate softmax-normalized dot products, operations that amplify even small analog perturbations into large functional errors. Mapping ViTs to crossbar arrays therefore sits at the intersection of the highest-value workload and the highest-risk substrate, making it an ideal probe for the practical limits of analog edge inference.

The gap between device-level promise and system-level performance is dominated by non-ideality. Analog memory cells suffer from cell-to-cell (C2C) variability, device-to-device (D2D) mismatch, retention drift, and—most critically for this study—nonlinear write dynamics. In a nonlinear write regime, the programmed conductance deviates from the target weight in a state-dependent fashion, distorting the effective linear transform that the crossbar is supposed to implement. Unlike additive thermal noise, write nonlinearity introduces multiplicative, input-dependent perturbations that standard training pipelines do not naturally absorb. Hardware-aware training (HAT) mitigates some of these effects by injecting device noise into the forward training path, yet the standard practice of fixing a single D2D mismatch map throughout training invites a subtler failure: the model memorizes one hardware instance rather than learning to generalize across the deployment distribution. When the trained checkpoint encounters a fresh fabricated array with a different fixed mismatch realization, accuracy can collapse to chance level. This hardware-instance overfitting is especially pernicious because it is invisible during conventional same-instance validation, where the model is tested on the same noisy realization it trained against.

Recent work has made meaningful progress on the mild-nonlinearity regime. The compute-ViT framework introduced a profile-driven behavioral simulation pipeline that maps literature-derived organic device parameters to task-level vision accuracy for ResNet, ConvNeXt, and Tiny-ViT backbones. Within that framework, ensemble hardware-aware training—resampling the D2D mismatch mask at every training epoch rather than reusing a fixed map—recovers strong fresh-instance accuracy on CIFAR-10 with Tiny-ViT under moderate write nonlinearity ($\text{NL}=1.0$). A literature-anchored zero-shot transfer to a calibrated organic device profile confirms the framework's predictive validity. These results establish that analog-mapped vision transformers can in principle achieve deployment-grade accuracy, provided the device stress remains within a certain envelope. What all of these positive results share is a shared assumption: the write nonlinearity is moderate enough that first-order gradient-scaling approximations remain valid during training. The success of ensemble HAT under $\text{NL}=1.0$ is genuine and important, but it does not answer whether the same recipe—or any known variant—survives once the nonlinearity intensifies.

Severe write nonlinearity breaks that assumption. When the nonlinearity parameter reaches $\text{NL}=2.0$, the same first-order HAT recipe that succeeds under $\text{NL}=1.0$ collapses on fresh hardware instances. This is not a gradual slope but a sharp boundary: the gradient-scaling surrogate, which linearizes the write distortion around the current weight estimate, no longer captures the curvature of the actual programming dynamics. Prior work has documented this boundary and correctly labeled it an approximation limit rather than a fundamental materials impossibility. What prior work has not done is subject it to rigorous falsification. The literature has proposed candidate mitigations—architectural linearization, block-heterogeneous training, and joint distributional resampling—but no study has independently tested these strategies under identical fresh-instance evaluation conditions to determine whether any of them can break the ceiling. Without such a test, the barrier could equally well be an optimization failure, a training-budget limitation, or a true structural incompatibility between severe nonlinearity and the attention mechanism. Distinguishing among these explanations is essential because each implies a radically different research investment: more epochs and hyperparameter search if the problem is optimization, higher-fidelity device models if the problem is surrogate mismatch, or entirely new attention architectures if the problem is structural.

This paper closes that gap by adopting an explicit falsification posture. We treat the severe-NL ceiling as a testable hypothesis and design three independent candidate mitigations that, if successful, would refute the claim that the barrier is structural. First, we linearize only the MLP blocks, which prior layer-sensitivity analysis identifies as the dominant noise bottleneck, while leaving the attention pathway exposed to full $\text{NL}=2.0$. Second, we linearize the entire model—both MLP and attention layers—to establish an architectural upper bound. Third, we combine MLP-linear protection with ensemble HAT and warm-start from the canonical $\text{NL}=1.0$ checkpoint, creating a joint training recipe that simultaneously addresses severe nonlinearity and instance overfitting. Each mitigation is evaluated under a standardized fresh-instance protocol: ten unseen D2D realizations, five Monte Carlo evaluations per realization, with primary metrics reported as mean ± standard deviation across instances. The compute-ViT framework supplies the common simulation substrate, ensuring that differences across arms reflect genuine capability boundaries rather than implementation variance. By testing three conceptually distinct escape routes, we maximize the chance of refutation and therefore maximize the epistemic value of a negative result.

The results falsify the MLP-dominance hypothesis. MLP-only linearization recovers strong source-domain accuracy but falls to approximately $32\%$ on fresh hardware. Full linearization performs almost identically. The joint recipe, which was pre-registered as the most promising path to deployment-grade fresh-instance accuracy under severe NL, converges to the same approximate $30\%$ ceiling. The convergence of three architecturally distinct strategies on the same fresh-instance regime is precisely the pattern one expects from a structural barrier rather than an optimization shortfall. If the barrier were merely a matter of insufficient training budget or suboptimal hyperparameters, at least one of the three arms—especially the joint recipe with its warm-start advantage—should have escaped the trap. That none do suggests the failure is built into the functional form of the attention pathway under first-order nonlinearity modeling.

To explain this convergence, we formulate the **Structural-Limit Hypothesis**: under first-order $\text{NL}=2.0$ surrogates, the QKV and output-projection gradients acquire a state-dependent asymmetry that couples the learned spatial filters to the particular D2D pattern experienced during training. When the D2D pattern changes at deployment, the attention geometry—which is the core representational engine of the transformer—ceases to function. The MLP path, even when perfectly linearized, cannot compensate for a broken attention mechanism. The hypothesis rests on three theoretical pillars: (i) gradient-asymmetry-induced rank collapse in Q/K projections; (ii) exponential amplification of surrogate bias by the softmax attention operator; and (iii) instance-specific scale-recovery mismatch that is invisible to source-domain accuracy but fatal to fresh-instance transfer. We derive falsifiable mathematical conditions for each pillar, ensuring that the hypothesis can be revised or refuted as new data arrive.

Our contributions are threefold:

1. **Formalization of the Structural-Limit Hypothesis.** We provide the first mathematically grounded statement of why severe write nonlinearity may constitute a structural generalization barrier in analog-mapped attention blocks. The three-pillar framework (rank collapse, exponential amplification, scale-recovery mismatch) transforms an empirical observation into a falsifiable theoretical claim with pre-registered conditions.

2. **Experimental falsification framework.** We design and execute three independent mitigations (MLP-only linearization, all-linear upper bound, joint MLP-linear + ensemble HAT) capable of refuting the structural hypothesis. Their convergence on the same fresh-instance ceiling provides strong converging negative evidence. All experiments are pre-registered, and the evaluation protocol is standardized across arms.

3. **Design principles for higher-order surrogates.** We derive a second-order Taylor-corrected straight-through estimator (STE) and a third-order cumulant expansion, framing them not as guaranteed breakthroughs but as falsification probes (CX-J1d). Whether these higher-order surrogates break the ceiling adjudicates between the "surrogate-dependent" and "architecture-dependent" interpretations of the barrier. We also specify the CX-J1b/c sub-path diagnostics to localize the bottleneck within the attention pathway.

**Scope delimitation.** This study is simulation-only, conducted on Tiny-ViT / CIFAR-10 at approximately five million parameters, and confined to the first-order gradient-scaling surrogate family. Our claims are about structural limits *within this surrogate class*, not absolute physical impossibility. If higher-order or physics-informed surrogates break the ceiling, the structural hypothesis is falsified in its strong form—a result that would be scientifically valuable in its own right.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work on analog CIM simulation, hardware-aware training, and nonlinearity mitigation. Section 3 details the compute-ViT framework, presents the structural-limit hypothesis and its three pillars, derives the second-order surrogate extension, and formalizes the falsifiable conditions. Section 4 describes the six core experiments of the CX-J diagnostic protocol, including pre-registered predictions. Section 5 discusses the implications of the structural ceiling for device engineering, surrogate design, and analog-CIM roadmapping. Section 6 concludes with actionable recommendations.
