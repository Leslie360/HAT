# Structural-Limit Hypothesis for Nonlinear-Write Vision Transformers

**Formal statement.** Let $f(\theta; D)$ be a Vision Transformer with parameters $\theta$ evaluated on device instance $D$. Let $L(\theta; D)$ be the test loss. Under a first-order nonlinear-write surrogate with $\text{NL} \ge 2.0$, there exists a threshold $\tau$ such that for all training procedures $T$ in the set $\{\text{fixed-mask}, \text{Ensemble HAT}, \text{MLP-linear}, \text{all-linear}, \text{joint}\}$, the expected fresh-instance accuracy satisfies
$$
\mathbb{E}_{D \sim P}\big[\text{acc}(T(\theta); D)\big] \le \tau \approx 30\%.
$$

**Proof sketch.** Consider a single attention head. Let the conductance nonlinearity map raw weights to effective conductances as $g \mapsto g^{\text{NL}}$. The attention score is
$$
a_{ij} = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)_{ij},
$$
where $Q = X W_Q$ and $K = X W_K$. Under the nonlinear-write surrogate, each matrix-vector product is implemented via crossbars whose effective conductances follow the power law $g^{\text{NL}}$. Consequently, the pre-softmax logits scale as $(QK^{\top})_{\text{eff}} \sim (X W_Q)^{\text{NL}} \cdot (X W_K)^{\text{NL}}$ rather than linearly. For $\text{NL}=2.0$, the dynamic range of the argument to softmax collapses by a factor $X = (\max g / \min g)^{\text{NL}} / (\max g / \min g) = (\max g / \min g)$, because the ratio of extreme conductances grows superlinearly while the available physical conductance window is fixed.

This collapse is irrecoverable by training because it occurs in the forward pass, not the backward pass. Gradient descent can only adjust $\theta$ so that $Q$ and $K$ produce logits that, after the *physical* nonlinearity, approximate the desired distribution. However, the nonlinearity is applied element-wise to conductances that are fixed at inference time; no update to $\theta$ can change the mapping from nominal weight to effective conductance. Even if the optimizer finds weights that compensate in the digital surrogate, the fresh instance $D$ samples a new realization of the conductance nonlinearity, so the learned compensation does not transfer.

MLP linearization does not affect the attention pathway, hence cannot change the bound. Replacing MLP activation functions with linear operations only removes nonlinearities in the feed-forward blocks; the self-attention mechanism still suffers from the conductance-power-law mapping described above. Since the accuracy bottleneck is driven by the collapse of attention scores, linearizing downstream components leaves the dominant source of information loss untouched. The bound $\tau$ therefore applies even to the all-linear and MLP-linear variants.

**Limitations of the proof sketch.** The argument is heuristic in several respects. First, it treats the dynamic-range collapse as a single scaling factor $X$ applied uniformly across all heads and layers, whereas the actual effect is layer-dependent and shaped by weight-magnitude distributions. Second, it assumes that the softmax output becomes approximately uniform when the dynamic range collapses, which is accurate only when the collapsed range is much smaller than the temperature $\sqrt{d_k}$; for partial collapse the effect on accuracy is an empirical question. Third, the proof relies on a first-order surrogate that replaces each MAC array with an independent conductance nonlinearity; higher-order parasitics (IR drop, source degeneration, sneak paths) could alter the mapping. Finally, the threshold $\tau \approx 30\%$ is drawn from observed experimental ceilings rather than derived ab initio.

**Connection to CX-J1b/c/d.** Each diagnostic experiment in the CX-J1 suite tests a distinct premise of the formal statement. CX-J1b (nonlinearity sweep) varies NL across chips to verify that the bound tightens as NL approaches 2.0. CX-J1c (layerwise linearization) isolates the attention pathway by linearizing MLPs while keeping self-attention intact; if accuracy remains near the ceiling, it confirms that the bottleneck is not in the feed-forward blocks. CX-J1d (ensemble HAT versus fixed mask) evaluates whether architectural remedies can escape the bound by decoupling subsets of parameters; if the expected fresh-instance accuracy stays below $\tau$, it supports the claim that the limit is structural rather than algorithmic.
