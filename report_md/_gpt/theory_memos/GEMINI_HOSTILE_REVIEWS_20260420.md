## Reviewer #A: The Methodology Purist

**Stance:** "I do not care about your conclusions until I trust your experimental protocol. Every simulation choice is suspect until validated against physical reality or at least against an independent simulator."

---

### Overall Assessment

This manuscript presents a simulation study of Vision Transformer (ViT) inference on analog in-memory computing (AIMC) arrays, with a focus on non-ideal write behavior. The central narrative — that severe write nonlinearity (NL = 2.0) imposes a ~30% "structural ceiling" on fresh-instance accuracy, and that ensemble hardware-aware training (HAT) rescues the moderate-NL regime — is interesting but **methodologically unsubstantiated**. The paper suffers from three fatal flaws that prevent me from recommending acceptance in any form.

**Flaw 1: The simulator is unvalidated.** The authors compare their framework against CrossSim on a 1,000-image subset and observe a 14.43 percentage-point divergence under noise injection (81.63% vs. 67.20% at σ=5%). They dismiss this as a "preliminary" sensitivity of the noise-to-conductance mapping. I do not accept this dismissal. A 14 pp gap on a standard benchmark (CIFAR-10) between two simulation frameworks is not a sensitivity; it is a **validity crisis**. If the authors' noise mapping is so plastic that it shifts accuracy by more than the effect size of their main claims, then none of their quantitative conclusions are trustworthy. The manuscript must either (a) reconcile the divergence with a root-cause analysis, or (b) retract all absolute accuracy numbers and report only relative rankings. It does neither.

**Flaw 2: The surrogate is both the problem and the solution.** The authors train with a first-order power-law surrogate (Φ(G) = |G|^NL, STE backward) and then argue that this surrogate is "fundamentally inadequate" for severe nonlinearity. This is circular. The structural-limit hypothesis is derived from data generated by the surrogate; the hypothesis then predicts that even the true physics would hit the ceiling. This is not science; it is **self-fulfilling prophecy**. The paper must demonstrate that the surrogate error is the dominant source of the ~30% ceiling, not merely assert it. A control experiment — training with the surrogate but evaluating on the true physical forward model (if available) or a higher-fidelity model — is absent.

**Flaw 3: The evaluation protocol is under-specified.** The "fresh-instance" evaluation resamples D2D mismatch, but the paper never states how many instances are drawn, whether the resampling is stratified by mismatch magnitude, or whether the reported mean ± std is over instances or over training seeds. The 86.37 ± 1.54% figure for Ensemble HAT (NL=1.0) looks precise, but without knowing the worst-case instance or the training-seed variance, it is uninterpretable. For all I know, the 1.54% std masks a bimodal distribution with one mode at 95% and another at 70%.

### Specific Questions

1. **CrossSim reconciliation (blocking):** The 14.43 pp divergence at σ=5% must be resolved. Which specific parameter in the noise-to-conductance mapping differs? Is it the scaling of σ relative to G_max, the handling of negative weights (differential pair mapping), or the quantization rounding mode? Provide a table of all mapping parameters for both frameworks.

2. **Surrogate validation (blocking):** How do we know the first-order surrogate is the cause of the ~30% ceiling? Train a model with NL = 2.0 using **ideal backward gradients** (i.e., simulate the forward distortion but back-propagate as if NL = 1.0). If fresh-instance accuracy recovers, the barrier is gradient-mismatch-driven. If it stays at ~30%, the barrier is forward-map-driven. This experiment is trivial to implement and would instantly clarify the mechanism.

3. **Ensemble HAT variance decomposition (major):** Report the variance components: Var(across training seeds) vs. Var(across D2D instances) vs. Cov(seed, instance). If training-seed variance is large, the 86.37% is not reproducible. If instance variance is large, the ensemble is not sufficiently diverse.

4. **ADC quantization modeling (minor):** The paper claims a 6-bit ADC cliff. Is the ADC modeled as uniform rounding, stochastic rounding, or learned quantization? The choice affects the cliff location. ReRAM literature often uses stochastic rounding, which shifts the cliff by 1–2 bits.

### Revision Requirements

- **R1:** Conduct and report the ideal-backward-gradient control experiment (Q2 above). This is non-negotiable.
- **R2:** Either reconcile the CrossSim divergence with a parameter-by-parameter comparison or remove the CrossSim comparison entirely and replace it with an internal ablation (e.g., varying the noise-mapping parameter over a 10× range and reporting ranking invariance).
- **R3:** Report per-instance accuracy distributions for all fresh-instance evaluations, not just mean ± std. Include minimum, maximum, and a histogram or violin plot.
- **R4:** Add a "Simulator Fidelity" table in the Methods section listing every modeled effect, its mathematical form, and its source (literature citation or measured data).

### Verdict

**Reject.** The methodological foundation is too unstable to support the theoretical claims. The surrogate circularity and the CrossSim divergence are not cosmetic issues; they undermine the epistemic status of every number in the paper. A major revision would require physical validation or at minimum a rigorous internal validation protocol. I am not convinced this can be achieved within the revision timeline of this venue.

## Reviewer #B: The Hardware Experimentalist

**Stance:** "I build chips. Your simulator is a toy. Until you show me measured organic-array data, your 'risk ranking' is fantasy. I am particularly allergic to energy numbers pulled from ReRAM papers."

---

### Overall Assessment

The authors have built an elaborate simulator for organic optoelectronic compute-in-memory (CIM) and used it to train Vision Transformers. As a hardware researcher who has fabricated and tested organic OECT arrays, I find the paper **technically naive about the target technology** and dangerously overconfident in its quantitative projections.

**Fatal concern: There is no hardware validation.** The entire paper — every accuracy number, every energy estimate, every risk ranking — is a simulation. The authors admit this in the Limitations section: "Validation against fabricated organic arrays is deferred to future work." In my field, this is not a limitation; it is a **disqualifier**. The organic devices the authors claim to model do not behave like power-law conductance mappings. Real organic OECTs exhibit:
- **Pronounced hysteresis:** The conductance after a write pulse depends on the history of previous pulses, not just the target weight. The paper's first-order surrogate has no memory.
- **Temperature sensitivity:** The photoresponse and mismatch variance shift by 10–20% over a 20°C range. The paper models temperature as absent.
- **Aging and drift:** Conductance states relax over seconds to minutes. The paper models a single 1,000 s retention test as a scalar placeholder.
- **Heavy-tailed D2D distributions:** The Gaussian D2D model is a convenient fiction. Real arrays show spatial gradients (wafer-level trends) and occasional defective devices (outliers at 5–10× the nominal σ).

The paper's response to these omissions is to list them in the Limitations paragraph. This is insufficient. A limitation is something that marginally degrades the result; an **omission of dominant physics** invalidates the result.

**Energy numbers are speculative.** The ~11× energy reduction versus FP32 is derived from ReRAM literature placeholders. Organic OECTs have fundamentally different programming mechanisms (electrochemical doping, ion migration) than ReRAM (filamentary switching). The energy per write pulse, the write latency, and the read energy are all unknown for the target technology. The authors multiply placeholder numbers together and present a ratio with two significant figures. This is **numerology**, not engineering.

**The ADC cliff is suspiciously convenient.** The authors claim that 6-bit ADC resolution marks a sharp transition. In real mixed-signal systems, ADC precision interacts with array nonlinearity: a highly nonlinear write produces conductance levels that are themselves non-uniform, so a 6-bit ADC may resolve fewer than 6 effective bits. The paper models ADC and NL independently. This is unrealistic.

### Specific Questions

1. **Hardware collaboration (blocking):** Do the authors have access to fabricated organic arrays? If not, why should the device community read this paper? If yes, why is no validation data included?

2. **Hysteresis modeling (major):** The first-order surrogate assumes deterministic, history-independent mapping. What is the quantitative error introduced by ignoring hysteresis? Even a toy model (e.g., Preisach hysteresis with 2–3 parameters) would demonstrate awareness of the issue.

3. **Energy accounting (major):** Provide a complete energy breakdown — not just "dense projections vs. digital attention" — but read energy, write energy, ADC energy, DAC energy, digital control overhead, and routing energy, with explicit placeholder flags for each. The current 11× figure is un-auditable.

4. **Defect-device tolerance (minor):** Real arrays have stuck-at faults and high-resistance outliers. How does the model behave if 1% of devices are stuck at G_min or G_max? This is a standard stress test in emerging-memory literature.

5. **Temperature and retention (minor):** The 1,000 s retention test is a single snapshot. Real deployment requires 10-year retention for some applications. What is the projected accuracy degradation under extrapolated drift models?

### Revision Requirements

- **R1:** Add a "Technology Realism" section that maps every simulator parameter to a measured or literature-derived range for organic OECTs specifically (not ReRAM). If a parameter is a placeholder, flag it with a symbol (†) and explain the sensitivity of the main claims to its value.
- **R2:** Remove the 11× energy claim from the abstract, introduction, and conclusion. It may remain in the Methods as a "scoping estimate" with explicit uncertainty bounds.
- **R3:** Model at least one non-ideal effect beyond the power-law surrogate: hysteresis, temperature dependence, or heavy-tailed D2D. Show that the ranking of HAT methods is either robust or fragile to this addition.
- **R4:** If hardware data truly cannot be included, the paper must be reframed as a **methodology contribution** ("a training protocol for AIMC simulation") rather than a **technology assessment** ("risk ranking for organic CIM"). The current framing is the latter and is therefore unsupported.

### Verdict

**Reject and encourage resubmission to a simulation/methodology venue.** The paper contains useful training ideas (Ensemble HAT is sensible), but the packaging as a "deployment risk ranking" for organic CIM is premature. I would consider a revised version that either (a) includes even minimal hardware validation (e.g., a single measured conductance distribution mapped to accuracy), or (b) explicitly limits its scope to simulator methodology and compares against other simulators (CrossSim, AIHWKit, NeuroSim) on equal footing. As it stands, the paper is a simulation study about a technology the authors have not touched.

## Reviewer #C: The Machine Learning Theorist

**Stance:** "I do not care about your devices. I care about whether your 'structural limit' is actually structural or just a training artifact. Your hypothesis is under-formalized, your proofs are absent, and your claims about transformers are empirically thin."

---

### Overall Assessment

This paper attempts to draw a theoretical conclusion — that severe write nonlinearity imposes a "structural ceiling" on transformer accuracy in analog memory — from empirical simulation data. As a theorist, I am sympathetic to the ambition but **deeply unsatisfied with the rigor**. The paper mixes three distinct genres (empirical ML, device simulation, and theoretical speculation) without mastering any of them. The result is a muddle: too formal for a systems paper, too sloppy for a theory paper.

**The structural-limit hypothesis is not a hypothesis; it is storytelling.** The authors define three "pillars" (gradient-asymmetry-induced rank collapse, exponential amplification by softmax, instance-specific scale-recovery mismatch) and then assert that these pillars "explain" the ~30% ceiling. But there are no proofs, no concentration inequalities, no scaling laws, and no PAC-Bayes bounds. The "prediction" about rank collapse (Pillar I) is not accompanied by any rank measurement. The "prediction" about KL divergence (Pillar II) is not accompanied by any attention-map analysis. The "prediction" about second-order correction (Pillar III) is an experiment that has not been run. A hypothesis that makes no predictions before the fact and retrofits three mechanisms after the fact is **post-hoc rationalization**.

**The attention-pathway-dominance claim lacks a control.** The authors claim that transformers are more fragile than CNNs because attention is "global and rank-sensitive." But the comparison is confounded: Tiny-ViT is fine-tuned from a pretrained checkpoint, while ConvNeXt is trained from scratch. A fair comparison would require training both from scratch under identical HAT protocols. Moreover, the MLP-Mixer control (mentioned in G-GG4 as CX-J1d) has not been run. Without it, the claim that fragility is transformer-specific is unsupported.

**The ~30% ceiling is likely a training hyperparameter issue.** The authors report that three independent mitigations converge to ~30%. I am not impressed by convergence; I am suspicious of it. When multiple independent interventions produce the same outcome, the common cause is often the **training protocol itself** (learning rate, weight decay, warmup schedule, batch size). The paper reports a single training configuration. What if the ceiling is simply the accuracy of a poorly regularized Tiny-ViT on CIFAR-100? Have the authors verified that a *digital* Tiny-ViT with the same training hyperparameters reaches >90%? The digital baseline is 98.06%, but that may use a different training recipe. If the HAT training recipe is suboptimal even in digital, the ~30% ceiling reflects training failure, not structural limitation.

**The ensemble training protocol is under-theorized.** The authors propose Ensemble HAT as a solution to hardware-instance overfitting but provide no learning-theoretic analysis. What is the Rademacher complexity of the ensemble-trained hypothesis class? How does the generalization bound depend on the number of D2D instances sampled per epoch? Does the ensemble objective in Eq. (2) satisfy uniform convergence? Without such analysis, Ensemble HAT is a heuristic, not a principled method.

### Specific Questions

1. **Formalization (blocking):** State the structural-limit hypothesis as a formal theorem with explicit assumptions. What is the function class? What is the loss? What is the data distribution? What does "ceiling" mean — a supremum over training procedures, a Bayes-optimal bound, or an empirical average? The current statement is too vague to be falsifiable.

2. **Rank-collapse evidence (major):** Measure the effective rank of W_Q and W_K under NL = 1.0 vs. NL = 2.0. Report the singular-value spectra. If the ranks are not lower under NL = 2.0, Pillar I is falsified and must be retracted.

3. **Training-recipe control (major):** Train the digital Tiny-ViT using the *exact same HAT training recipe* (same optimizer, same schedule, same augmentation) but with ideal weights (NL = 1.0, no D2D, no C2C). Report the accuracy. If it is <95%, the HAT recipe is suboptimal and the ~30% ceiling may be a training artifact.

4. **Architecture control (major):** Run the MLP-Mixer or a pure MLP architecture under the same NL = 2.0 protocol. If it also hits ~30%, the barrier is not transformer-specific. If it exceeds 50%, the transformer-specific claim is supported. This experiment is cheap and decisive.

5. **Generalization bound for Ensemble HAT (minor):** Provide a PAC-Bayes or uniform-convergence bound for the ensemble objective. Even a loose bound would demonstrate theoretical awareness. If the bound is vacuous, say so.

### Revision Requirements

- **R1:** Demote the structural-limit hypothesis from a "hypothesis" to a "conjecture" or "empirical observation." Remove language like "fundamental generalization barrier" and "structural ceiling." Replace with "convergence plateau under the tested conditions."
- **R2:** Add the digital-HAT-control experiment (Q3) and the MLP-Mixer control (Q4). These are minimal bars for any architecture-specific claim.
- **R3:** Report rank measurements and attention-map KL divergences from existing checkpoints. Do not claim theoretical mechanisms without empirical correlates.
- **R4:** If the authors wish to make theoretical claims, add a Theory section with formal definitions, assumptions, and at least one lemma with proof. Alternatively, remove all theoretical claims and submit as a pure empirical systems paper.

### Verdict

**Major revision.** The empirical observations (Ensemble HAT improves transfer) are potentially valuable, but they are smothered by overreaching theoretical claims. I recommend the authors choose one of two paths:
- **Path A (Theory):** Formalize the structural-limit claim with proofs, assumptions, and falsification criteria. This would be suitable for a theory venue (COLT, ICML theory track).
- **Path B (Systems):** Strip the theory, keep the empirical training protocols, and compare exhaustively against baseline HAT methods. This would be suitable for a systems venue (MLSys, ISCA, ISSCC).

The current draft is a Frankenstein: a systems paper with a theory tumor. I cannot recommend acceptance until the genre is clarified and the controls are added.
