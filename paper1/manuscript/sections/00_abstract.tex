\begin{abstract}
Analog compute-in-memory (CIM) can reduce movement-dominated inference cost, but deployment depends on two separable questions: whether a trained network transfers across hardware instances and whether the programmed memory state remains accurate over time. We present a behavioral simulation framework that decouples these questions for low-precision vision transformers. In an idealized AIHWKit IdealDevice ablation, standard fixed-instance hardware-aware training reaches 87.28\% fresh-instance accuracy at 8-bit precision but falls to 14.64\% at 4-bit; Ensemble Hardware-Aware Training (HAT), which resamples device-to-device (D2D) mismatch masks during training, restores the 4-bit pure-quantization ablation to 86.16\%. Reintroducing realistic phase-change-memory (PCM) UnitCell physics produces a different precision-retention frontier: 8-bit PCM gives the highest fresh accuracy (77.60\%) with negligible drift, 4-bit PCM remains competitive immediately after programming (76.68\%) but violates a 1~pp deployment-drift SLA after a 4.01~pp one-day loss, and 6-bit PCM enters a D2D-sensitive transition zone with lower mean accuracy (68.44\%, seed-level std 6.03\%) despite drift stability ($<$0.1~pp). Thus the algorithmic result is not itself a PCM deployment claim. Ensemble HAT addresses cross-instance robustness, whereas the deployable PCM operating point is governed by retention and quantization--D2D interactions.
\end{abstract}
