# Introduction

Compute-in-memory (CIM) architectures eliminate the data-movement bottleneck that constrains conventional digital inference by executing dense matrix-vector multiplications directly inside memory arrays. For edge vision workloads, where energy budgets are measured in milliwatts and latency budgets in milliseconds, this paradigm offers a plausible path to orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains. Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged as the architecture of choice for many visual tasks, delivering competitive accuracy with global receptive fields and parameter-efficient scaling. Yet ViTs are also among the most demanding models to deploy on analog substrates: their self-attention mechanisms rely on high-fidelity query-key-value transformations and delicate softmax-normalized dot products, operations that amplify even small analog perturbations into large functional errors. Mapping ViTs to crossbar arrays therefore sits at the intersection of the highest-value workload and the highest-risk substrate, making it an ideal probe for the practical limits of analog edge inference.

The gap between device-level promise and system-level performance is dominated by non-ideality. Analog memory cells suffer from cell-to-cell (C2C) variability, device-to-device (D2D) mismatch, retention drift, and—most critically for this study—nonlinear write dynamics. In a nonlinear write regime, the programmed conductance deviates from the target weight in a state-dependent fashion, distorting the effective linear transform that the crossbar is supposed to implement. Unlike additive thermal noise, write nonlinearity introduces multiplicative, input-dependent perturbations that standard training pipelines do not naturally absorb. Hardware-aware training (HAT) mitigates some of these effects by injecting device noise into the forward training path, yet the standard practice of fixing a single D2D mismatch map throughout training invites a subtler failure: the model memorizes one hardware instance rather than learning to generalize across the deployment distribution. When the trained checkpoint encounters a fresh fabricated array with a different fixed mismatch realization, accuracy can collapse to chance level. This hardware-instance overfitting is especially pernicious because it is invisible during conventional same-instance validation, where the model is tested on the same noisy realization it trained against.

Recent work has made meaningful progress on the mild-nonlinearity regime. The compute-ViT framework introduced a profile-driven behavioral simulation pipeline that maps literature-derived organic device parameters to task-level vision accuracy for ResNet, ConvNeXt, and Tiny-ViT backbones. Within that framework, ensemble hardware-aware training—resampling the D2D mismatch mask at every training epoch rather than reusing a fixed map—recovers 86.37 ± 1.54 % fresh-instance accuracy on CIFAR-10 with Tiny-ViT under moderate write nonlinearity (NL = 1.0). A literature-anchored zero-shot transfer to a calibrated OPECT device profile reaches 88.53 ± 0.08 %. These results establish that analog-mapped vision transformers can in principle achieve deployment-grade accuracy, provided the device stress remains within a certain envelope. Iso-accuracy contour analysis further shows that ADC precision rather than conductance quantization becomes the binding constraint below six bits, while D2D mismatch governs degradation above that threshold. What all of these positive results share is a shared assumption: the write nonlinearity is moderate enough that first-order gradient-scaling approximations remain valid during training. The success of ensemble HAT under NL = 1.0 is genuine and important, but it does not answer whether the same recipe—or any known variant—survives once the nonlinearity intensifies.

Severe write nonlinearity breaks that assumption. When the nonlinearity parameter reaches NL = 2.0, the same first-order HAT recipe that recovers 86.37 % under NL = 1.0 collapses to 27.72 ± 0.82 % on fresh hardware instances. This is not a gradual slope but a sharp boundary: the gradient-scaling surrogate, which linearizes the write distortion around the current weight estimate, no longer captures the curvature of the actual programming dynamics. Prior work has documented this boundary and correctly labeled it an approximation limit rather than a fundamental materials impossibility. What prior work has not done is subject it to rigorous falsification. The literature has proposed candidate mitigations—architectural linearization, block-heterogeneous training, and joint distributional resampling—but no study has independently tested these strategies under identical fresh-instance evaluation conditions to determine whether any of them can break the ceiling. Without such a test, the 30 % barrier could equally well be an optimization failure, a training-budget limitation, or a true structural incompatibility between severe nonlinearity and the attention mechanism. Distinguishing among these explanations is essential because each implies a radically different research investment: more epochs and hyperparameter search if the problem is optimization, higher-fidelity device models if the problem is surrogate mismatch, or entirely new attention architectures if the problem is structural.

This paper closes that gap by adopting an explicit falsification posture. We treat the severe-NL ceiling as a testable hypothesis and design three independent candidate mitigations that, if successful, would refute the claim that the barrier is structural. First, we linearize only the MLP blocks, which prior layer-sensitivity analysis identifies as the dominant noise bottleneck, while leaving the attention pathway exposed to full NL = 2.0. Second, we linearize the entire model—both MLP and attention layers—to establish an architectural upper bound. Third, we combine MLP-linear protection with ensemble HAT and warm-start from the canonical NL = 1.0 checkpoint, creating a joint training recipe that simultaneously addresses severe nonlinearity and instance overfitting. Each mitigation is evaluated under a standardized fresh-instance protocol: ten unseen D2D realizations, five Monte Carlo evaluations per realization, with primary metrics reported as mean ± standard deviation. The compute-ViT framework supplies the common simulation substrate, ensuring that differences across arms reflect genuine capability boundaries rather than implementation variance. By testing three conceptually distinct escape routes, we maximize the chance of refutation and therefore maximize the epistemic value of a negative result.

The results falsify all three hypotheses. MLP-only linearization recovers 87.79 % on the source training instance but falls to 32.12 ± 7.72 % on fresh hardware. Full linearization performs almost identically at 32.60 ± 9.18 %. The joint recipe, which was pre-registered as the most promising path to 80 % fresh-instance accuracy under severe NL, reaches 30.53 ± 7.07 %. The convergence of three architecturally distinct strategies on the same approximate 30 % ceiling is precisely the pattern one expects from a structural barrier rather than an optimization shortfall. If the barrier were merely a matter of insufficient training budget or suboptimal hyperparameters, at least one of the three arms—especially the joint recipe with its warm-start advantage—should have escaped the trap. That none do suggests the failure is built into the functional form of the attention pathway under first-order nonlinearity modeling. We trace the mechanism to the multiplicative query-key interactions in self-attention: even when MLP blocks are protected by linear surrogates, the attention operation propagates unmodeled curvature from the NL = 2.0 write dynamics in a way that first-order training gradients cannot correct.

Our contributions are threefold:

- **First rigorous falsification of severe-NL mitigation strategies for CIM-ViT.** We do not merely report another failed training run; we pre-register three independent mitigations, each capable of breaking the hypothesized ceiling, and show that all three converge on the same fresh-instance limit. This design follows the Popperian principle that a scientific claim earns its status by exposing itself to genuine risk of refutation. The negative result is therefore as informative as a positive one would have been.

- **Identification of a ~30 % fresh-instance structural ceiling.** The systematic convergence across MLP-only, all-linear, and joint-training arms provides evidence that the barrier is not an artifact of insufficient training data, suboptimal hyperparameters, or architectural under-parameterization, but a structural consequence of the interaction between first-order nonlinearity surrogates and the attention mechanism. This ceiling sets a concrete benchmark against which future device-engineering and surrogate-modeling advances can be measured.

- **Diagnostic protocol (CX-J1b/c/d) for future work.** We specify three targeted falsification experiments—QKV-only linearization, full-attention linearization, and higher-order Taylor surrogate replacement—that future researchers can use to determine whether the barrier persists under refined modeling assumptions. If any of these experiments breaks the ceiling, the structural hypothesis is refuted and an engineering pathway emerges; if none do, the hypothesis is strengthened and the field should invest in higher-fidelity write dynamics rather than training-recipe tweaks.
