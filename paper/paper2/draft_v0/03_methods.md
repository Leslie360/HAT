# Methods

## Framework Overview

All experiments are executed within the compute-ViT simulation framework, a PyTorch-native instrument for profile-driven analog CIM accuracy evaluation that we introduced in our companion work \citep{computevit2025}. The framework replaces standard `nn.Linear` and `nn.Conv2d` layers with drop-in analog equivalents (`AnalogLinear`, `AnalogConv2d`) that implement the full physical pipeline: float weight $\to$ differential conductance pair $(G^{+}, G^{-})$ $\to$ uniform quantization via straight-through estimator (STE) $\to$ fixed D2D noise injection $\to$ per-forward C2C noise resampling $\to$ optional retention decay $\to$ effective weight recovery with scale calibration. The differential-pair mapping, quantization scheme, and scale-recovery equations follow the specification detailed in \S3.1 of the companion paper. Framework outputs have been cross-validated against CrossSim GPU-accelerated crossbar simulation \citep{crosssim2026} on ResNet-18 and Tiny-ViT accuracy traces; the maximum discrepancy across 100 random inputs is $<0.3$ percentage points, confirming that the first-order behavioral surrogate is numerically faithful for the regime tested here. Device-specific parameters are supplied through a JSON profile interface; substituting a profile requires no code changes beyond loading a new parameter bundle.

## Device Profile

The canonical organic profile used in this study is anchored to dinaphtho-thieno-thiophene (DNTT) transient measurements \citep{vincze2025dualplasticity}. The profile specifies a 4-bit conductance resolution ($n_{\text{states}} = 16$) over a dynamic range $G_{\max}/G_{\min} = 10$ ($G_{\min} = 1.0$ a.u., $G_{\max} = 10.0$ a.u.), cycle-to-cycle variability $\sigma_{\text{C2C}} = 5\%$, and device-to-device mismatch $\sigma_{\text{D2D}} = 10\%$, both relative to the full conductance span. Noise is injected in uniform mode: $\mathcal{N}(0, (\sigma \cdot (G_{\max}-G_{\min}))^{2})$. Two write-nonlinearity regimes are compared. The *mild* regime sets $NL_{\text{LTP}} = 1.0$ and $NL_{\text{LTD}} = -1.0$, which recovers ideal linear write under the first-order surrogate. The *severe* regime sets $NL_{\text{LTP}} = 2.0$ and $NL_{\text{LTD}} = -2.0$, corresponding to a strongly sub-linear conductance update that prior array-level studies identify as a practical upper bound for organic RRAM programming fidelity \citep{zhang2026opect}. All other profile fields—retention, physical front-end, ADC distortion—are disabled unless explicitly noted, so that observed accuracy differences are attributable to the nonlinearity manipulation alone.

## Nonlinearity Surrogate

The first-order gradient-scaling surrogate modifies the STE backward pass by state-dependent factors that approximate the effect of nonlinear conductance programming on weight-update magnitudes. During forward inference, quantization proceeds identically to the ideal case: the weight is clamped to $[x_{\min}, x_{\max}]$, normalized, rounded to $n_{\text{states}}$ discrete levels, and denormalized. In the backward pass, the straight-through gradient $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial Q$ is rescaled by a piecewise factor that depends on the present conductance magnitude and the update direction (potentiation or depression). Concretely, let $\Delta G = x_{\max} - x_{\min}$ and let $\tilde{x}$ denote the clamped quantizer input saved during the forward pass. The surrogate gradient is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \tilde{x}} =
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Q} \times
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle \Bigl(\frac{x_{\max} - \tilde{x}}{\Delta G}\Bigr)^{NL_{\text{LTP}} - 1}, & \displaystyle \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Q} \geq 0, \\[12pt]
\displaystyle \Bigl(\frac{\tilde{x} - x_{\min}}{\Delta G}\Bigr)^{|NL_{\text{LTD}}| - 1}, & \displaystyle \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Q} < 0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:nl-surrogate}
\end{equation}
For $NL_{\text{LTP}} = 1.0$ and $|NL_{\text{LTD}}| = 1.0$, both exponents vanish and the identity gradient is recovered. For $NL_{\text{LTP}} = 2.0$, potentiation gradients near $x_{\max}$ are attenuated proportionally to the remaining headroom $(x_{\max} - \tilde{x}) / \Delta G$, mimicking the physical saturation of conductance increase at high programmed states. The factor is applied independently to each scalar entry of the weight tensor, making the surrogate cheap to evaluate and fully compatible with mixed-precision training.

The surrogate is deliberately first-order: it scales gradients but does not alter the forward quantization levels themselves. Higher-order curvature—arising from the true conductance-update trajectory, from programming-history dependence, or from cross-point interactions such as IR drop and sneak paths—is not captured. We treat the $NL = 2.0$ boundary as a limit of the surrogate class rather than as a universal materials impossibility. If future higher-fidelity models (e.g., second-order Taylor expansions, physics-based compact models, or measured pulse-response curves) break the ceiling reported here, the structural hypothesis advanced in this paper is falsified and the field should invest in surrogate redesign.

## HAT Taxonomy and Cadence

Hardware-aware training (HAT) injects device non-idealities into the forward optimization path while back-propagating through the STE. Three cadences are distinguished. *Fixed-mask HAT* samples one D2D mismatch map $M_{0} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\text{D2D}}^{2})$ at model initialization and holds it constant for all epochs; this is the standard practice in the CIM literature \citep{joshi2020accurate}. *Per-epoch Ensemble HAT* resamples a fresh map $M^{(e)}$ at the beginning of every epoch, holding it constant across all mini-batches within that epoch \citep{computevit2025}. *Per-batch HAT* resamples $M$ before every forward pass, exposing the optimizer to the highest-frequency device variation.

Cadence is an implicit design choice whose consequences are often overlooked. Fixed-mask HAT minimizes optimization variance but causes the model to overfit to a single hardware instance; when the trained checkpoint is deployed on a fresh fabricated array, accuracy collapses. Per-batch HAT destroys convergence because the loss landscape changes too rapidly for gradient descent to track a stable basin; in pilot experiments, training loss diverges within ten epochs. Per-epoch Ensemble HAT strikes the necessary balance: the mismatch map is stationary long enough for the optimizer to make meaningful progress, yet the epoch boundary provides sufficient distributional breadth to prevent instance memorization. All experiments in this study therefore use per-epoch resampling unless explicitly labeled otherwise. The D2D map is drawn as an independent Gaussian per crossbar element; spatial correlation structures are reserved for the robustness checks reported in the Supplementary Information.

## Fresh-Instance Evaluation Protocol

Training-array evaluation—testing a checkpoint on the same D2D realization it trained against—is dishonest for deployment scenarios because every fabricated chip presents a unique fixed mismatch pattern. We enforce a *fresh-instance protocol* that severs this link. After training completes, the checkpoint is loaded into a newly initialized model, the fixed D2D buffers are resampled with a fresh random seed, and evaluation proceeds on the new hardware instance. This procedure is repeated for $n = 10$ independent D2D realizations. On each instance, five Monte Carlo forward passes are executed with C2C noise resampled per pass; the instance mean is the average accuracy across these five passes. The primary reported metric is the mean and standard deviation across the ten instance means. Reporting instance-level variance rather than pooled forward-pass variance is essential because C2C noise averages down with repeated passes, whereas D2D mismatch is the dominant deployment uncertainty.

The protocol is implemented as follows. For instance $i \in \{0, \dots, 9\}$, the random seed is set to $42 + 100i$; this deterministic offset ensures reproducibility while guaranteeing independence across instances. The model architecture and checkpoint weights are reloaded, `resample_d2d_noise()` is called on every `AnalogLinear` and `AnalogConv2d` module to redraw the fixed mismatch buffers, and `set_noise_for_eval()` pushes the evaluation noise parameters into each layer. The test set is CIFAR-10 (10 000 images, 10 classes). No test-time augmentation, model averaging, or checkpoint ensembling is applied. Because the training D2D pattern is never reused at evaluation, the protocol measures true zero-shot hardware transfer rather than optimistic same-instance performance.

## Mitigation Strategies Tested

Three conceptually distinct mitigation lanes are evaluated under the severe-NL ($NL_{\text{LTP}} = 2.0$, $|NL_{\text{LTD}}| = 2.0$) regime, plus one positive control.

**MLP-linear.** Layer-sensitivity analysis in our prior work \citep{computevit2025} identified the MLP blocks as the dominant analog footprint (3.14 M parameters, 66 \% of analog weight count). The MLP-linear hypothesis posits that protecting these blocks from severe nonlinearity while leaving the attention pathway exposed should break the accuracy ceiling. In this lane, `NL_LTP` and `NL_LTD` are set to 1.0 for all layers whose module name contains `.mlp.fc1` or `.mlp.fc2`; all other analog layers—including QKV projection, attention output projection, and patch embedding—retain $NL = 2.0$. Training follows the standard V4 recipe: 100 epochs, AdamW ($lr = 5\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $0.05$), cosine annealing, batch size 64, with per-epoch D2D resampling active.

**All-linear.** If nonlinearity anywhere in the analog path is the sole bottleneck, linearizing every analog block should establish an architectural upper bound. In this lane, $NL = 1.0$ is enforced globally for all `AnalogLinear` and `AnalogConv2d` modules while preserving all other severe-regime settings ($\sigma_{\text{D2D}} = 10\%$, $\sigma_{\text{C2C}} = 5\%$, 16 conductance states). This is not a realistic deployment strategy—organic devices do not suddenly become linear—but it serves as a diagnostic: if even full linearization fails to restore fresh-instance accuracy, the barrier cannot be attributed to the MLP blocks alone and must involve the interaction between D2D mismatch and the attention mechanism.

**Joint MLP-linear + Ensemble HAT warm-start.** The third lane combines MLP-linear protection with the most robust training recipe known in the mild-NL regime. The checkpoint is warm-started from the canonical Ensemble HAT model trained at $NL = 1.0$ (the positive control), then fine-tuned for 100 epochs with MLP blocks linearized and attention blocks at $NL = 2.0$, using per-epoch D2D resampling throughout. Warm-starting provides the optimizer with a weight basin already proven to generalize across device instances, thereby maximizing the probability of escape from the severe-NL ceiling. If any training intervention can break the barrier, this joint recipe is the most plausible candidate because it simultaneously addresses block localization (MLP-linear) and instance overfitting (Ensemble HAT + warm-start).

**Control: Ensemble HAT at $NL = 1.0$.** The positive control retrains Tiny-ViT V4 under the canonical mild-nonlinearity profile ($NL_{\text{LTP}} = 1.0$, $|NL_{\text{LTD}}| = 1.0$, all other parameters identical) with per-epoch D2D resampling. This checkpoint validates that the framework, the protocol, and the fresh-instance evaluation pipeline are operational: it should reproduce the $86.37 \pm 1.54$ \% fresh-instance accuracy established in \citep{computevit2025}. Any failure to replicate this control would indicate a regression in the simulation stack rather than a genuine device limit.

## Statistical Treatment

All fresh-instance reports use $n = 10$ independent D2D realizations. The standard error of the mean is $\text{SEM} = \sigma / \sqrt{n}$, where $\sigma$ is the sample standard deviation across the ten instance means. Effect sizes between lanes are quantified with Cohen's $d$:
\begin{equation}
d = \frac{\bar{x}_{1} - \bar{x}_{2}}{s_{p}}, \qquad s_{p} = \sqrt{\frac{(n_{1}-1)s_{1}^{2} + (n_{2}-1)s_{2}^{2}}{n_{1}+n_{2}-2}},
\label{eq:cohens-d}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{x}_{i}$ and $s_{i}$ are the sample mean and standard deviation of lane $i$. A converging-ceiling claim requires not merely overlapping confidence intervals but also a small effect size between the mitigation lanes. Power analysis for a two-sample $t$-test with $\alpha = 0.05$ and target power $1 - \beta = 0.80$ indicates that $n = 10$ per arm is sufficient to detect a $7$ percentage point accuracy difference at the observed cross-instance standard deviation ($\sigma \approx 7$–$9$ pp). Welch's $t$-test is used when variance homogeneity is violated. All $p$-values are two-tailed.
