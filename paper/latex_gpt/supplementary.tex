\subsubsection{Nonlinear-Write Surrogate}
\label{subsec:supp-nl-surrogate}

In the surrogate model, nonlinear write modifies the STE backward pass according to the present conductance state:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial L}{\partial G}
    =
    \begin{cases}
        \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}}
        \left(\dfrac{G_{\max}-G}{G_{\max}-G_{\min}}\right)^{NL_{\mathrm{LTP}}-1},
        & \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}} \ge 0 \\[10pt]
        \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}}
        \left(\dfrac{G-G_{\min}}{G_{\max}-G_{\min}}\right)^{|NL_{\mathrm{LTD}}|-1},
        & \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}} < 0
    \end{cases}
\label{eq:supp-nl-surrogate}
\end{equation}
The absence of an explicit $NL$ prefactor in Eq.~\ref{eq:supp-nl-surrogate} is intentional: the surrogate scales the STE by the normalized conductance position, modeling state-dependent update difficulty (weaker updates near the bounds), rather than by the derivative of a power-law function $f(G)=G^{NL}$.
This is therefore a behavioral proxy for position-dependent gradient attenuation, not a strict functional derivative.
When \texttt{use\_second\_order\_ste=True}, a second-order Taylor correction adds a negative-signed brake to both branches, preventing aggressive bound-pushing without changing the first-order scaling.\footnote{Branch~A ratified in canonical commit \texttt{ab56c2d}.}
Ideal write is recovered when $NL_{\mathrm{LTP}}=1$ and $|NL_{\mathrm{LTD}}|=1$.


\subsection{Mathematical Formalism of Hardware-Aware Training}
\label{subsec:supp-hat-math}

Standard hardware-aware training (HAT) optimizes weights $\theta$ against a single, fixed device-to-device (D2D) mismatch field $M_0$:
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\mathrm{std}}^{\star}=\arg\min_{\theta}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\ell\!\left(f(x_{n};\theta,M_{0}),y_{n}\right),
\label{eq:hat-standard}
\end{equation}
Ensemble HAT replaces the fixed mask with samples from the D2D distribution:
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\mathrm{ens}}^{\star}=\arg\min_{\theta}\mathbb{E}_{M\sim p(M)}
    \left[\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\ell\!\left(f(x_{n};\theta,M),y_{n}\right)\right].
\label{eq:hat-ensemble-distribution}
\end{equation}
Fresh-instance transfer is then evaluated over unseen arrays $M'$ drawn from the same distribution:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{fresh}}=\mathbb{E}_{M'\sim p(M)}\!\left[\mathrm{Acc}\!\left(f(\cdot;\theta_{\mathrm{ens}}^{\star},M')\right)\right].
\label{eq:fresh-instance}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Physical Front-end Modeling}
\label{subsec:supp-frontend-math}

The organic phototransistor front end is modeled with an inverse-gamma preprocessor $P_{\mathrm{in}}$ applied to the normalized input $X \in [0,1]$:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\mathrm{in}} = X^{1/\gamma_{\mathrm{phys}}}.
\label{eq:inverse-gamma}
\end{equation}
The photocurrent $I_{\mathrm{photo}}$ subsequently incorporates physical transduction nonlinearity and shot noise $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{shot}}$:
\begin{equation}
    I_{\mathrm{photo}} = \alpha P_{\mathrm{in}}^{\gamma_{\mathrm{phys}}} + I_{\mathrm{dark}} + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{shot}} \approx \alpha X + I_{\mathrm{dark}} + \varepsilon_{\mathrm{shot}},
\label{eq:frontend-photoresponse}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon_{\mathrm{shot}}] \propto I_{\mathrm{photo}}$. The signal is renormalized per sample before being passed to the first neural layer:
\begin{equation}
    X_{\mathrm{norm}} = \frac{I_{\mathrm{photo}}-I_{\min}}{I_{\max}-I_{\min}+\epsilon}.
\label{eq:frontend-renorm}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Detailed Experiment Matrix}
\label{subsec:detailed-experiment-matrix}

Tiny-ViT experiments span a digital baseline, a quantized hybrid control, a fixed-mask noisy deployment baseline, a uniform-noise HAT model, and several physical-extension studies. For brevity, these regimes are denoted V1--V8 throughout the Supplementary Information. V3 uses fixed D2D masking, analogous to ConvNeXt C3, while V7--V8 represent early retention-aware explorations excluded from the main analysis.

Four physical-extension studies probe richer device regimes on CIFAR-10:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Proportional-noise HAT} (\texttt{V4\_proportional\_HAT}) repeats V4 for \(100\) epochs with state-dependent proportional noise and uses \(10\)-run Monte Carlo sweeps under both proportional and uniform-noise semantics.
    \item \textbf{Nonlinear-write HAT} (\texttt{V4\_NL2\_HAT}) repeats V4 for \(100\) epochs with nonlinear-write dynamics active during STE gradient computation (\(\mathrm{NL}_{\mathrm{LTP}}=+2.0\), \(\mathrm{NL}_{\mathrm{LTD}}=-2.0\)) and uses the same \(10\)-run Monte Carlo evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Ensemble HAT} (\texttt{V4\_Ensemble\_HAT}) keeps the canonical V4 recipe but resamples the fixed D2D realization at each epoch; transferability is then reported on \(10\) fresh hardware instances with \(5\) Monte Carlo evaluations per instance.
    \item \textbf{ConvNeXt proportional-noise HAT} (\texttt{C4}) applies the same proportional-noise HAT protocol to ConvNeXt-Tiny for \(200\) epochs as a cross-architecture comparison.
\end{itemize}
These runs are treated as physical-extension studies rather than as part of the grouped cross-dataset comparisons in the main text.

\subsubsection{Vincze et al. 2025 Parameter Extraction}

Because Vincze et al.\ \citep{vincze2025dualplasticity} is currently available as a 2025 Early Access paper, we list the extracted parameters used to define the canonical organic profile explicitly here:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Conductance Window}: $G_{\max}/G_{\min} \approx 10$, anchored to the scalable operation regime.
    \item \textbf{Retention Time Constants}: The dual-exponential fit for temporal drift yields a fast decay constant $\tau_1 = 140$~ms, a slow decay constant $\tau_2 = 610$~ms, and an initial amplitude ratio $A_0 = 0.6$. These align with the short-term and long-term plasticity dynamics reported for the device.
    \item \textbf{Nonlinear Write Asymmetry ($NL$)}: While the canonical profile uses symmetric writes ($NL=1.0$), the physical profile derived from the literature indicates moderate write nonlinearity corresponding to $NL_{LTP} = 2.0$ and $NL_{LTD} = -2.0$. In our simulations, $NL=2.0$ maps to a conductance saturation asymmetry (LTP vs. LTD magnitude at the window midpoint) of approximately 3:1, a regime consistent with reported long-pulse dynamics in DNTT-based organic synaptic transistors \citep{vincze2025dualplasticity}. We evaluate this directly in our severe stress tests.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Supplementary Methods}
\label{subsec:supp-methods}

\subsubsection{Differential Conductance Mapping Details}

Each analog-mapped weight tensor is converted into a differential-pair conductance representation over a bounded physical range $[G_{\min}, G_{\max}]$. The positive and negative branches are quantized to $n_{\mathrm{states}}$ levels with a straight-through estimator (STE), and the effective analog weight is recovered from the differential conductance. The layer-wise digital scale recovery is
\[
\hat{W}_{ij}=s_{\ell}G_{\mathrm{eff},ij},
\qquad
s_{\ell}=\frac{w_{\max}}{\Delta G},
\qquad
\Delta G = G_{\max}-G_{\min},
\]
with the optional retention-time recalibration
\[
s_{\ell}^{\mathrm{ret}}=\frac{w_{\max}}{\max_{i,j}|G_{\mathrm{eff},ij}|+\epsilon}.
\]
The recovered weight step is therefore
\[
\Delta_{\hat{W}} = s_{\ell}\frac{\Delta G}{n_{\mathrm{states}}-1},
\]
so the scale-masking regime discussed in the main text corresponds to perturbations satisfying $|s_{\ell}\delta G_{\mathrm{eff},ij}| < \Delta_{\hat{W}}/2$. We model this scale recovery as ideal calibrated digital post-processing rather than as an explicit physical peripheral circuit. Deterministic positive/negative branch asymmetry is not separately parameterized beyond the sampled D2D/C2C perturbations; any perfect common-mode cancellation implied by the differential form is a simplifying layout-level assumption.

\subsubsection{Variability and Write Dynamics Modeling}

Device-to-device (D2D) mismatch is modeled as a fixed Gaussian perturbation sampled once at initialization,
\[
\delta g_{\mathrm{D2D}} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \sigma_{\mathrm{D2D}}\Delta G\right),
\qquad
\Delta G = G_{\max}-G_{\min},
\]
whereas cycle-to-cycle (C2C) variation is resampled on each forward pass as
\[
\delta g_{\mathrm{C2C}} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \sigma_{\mathrm{C2C}}\Delta G\right).
\]
In proportional mode, the same Gaussian law is used but the perturbation magnitude scales with the instantaneous conductance state, i.e.\ $\delta g \propto |G|$ rather than $\Delta G$.

Nonlinear write modeling utilizes \texttt{NL\_LTP} and \texttt{NL\_LTD} coefficients to scale the STE backward pass. The surrogate follows
\[
\frac{\partial L}{\partial G}
=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}}
\left(\dfrac{G_{\max}-G}{G_{\max}-G_{\min}}\right)^{NL_{\mathrm{LTP}}-1},
& \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}} \ge 0 \\[10pt]
\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}}
\left(\dfrac{G-G_{\min}}{G_{\max}-G_{\min}}\right)^{|NL_{\mathrm{LTD}}|-1},
& \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \tilde{G}} < 0
\end{cases}
\]
so $NL_{\mathrm{LTP}}=1$ and $|NL_{\mathrm{LTD}}|=1$ recover the identity STE. Retention is implemented as a decay factor applied to the programmed conductance above the floor $G_{\min}$:
\[
G(t)=G_{\min}+\left(G_{\mathrm{prog}}-G_{\min}\right)\,f_{\mathrm{ret}}(t),
\]
with
\[
f_{\mathrm{ret}}(t)=A_0 + A_1 e^{-t/\tau_1} + A_2 e^{-t/\tau_2},
\qquad
A_1=A_2=\frac{1-A_0}{2},
\]
and parameters anchored to measured DNTT transients \citep{vincze2025dualplasticity}. In the optional state-dependent extension, the same form is used with conductance-dependent effective time constants so that high-conductance states decay modestly faster.

\subsubsection{Energy Profiler Implementation}

The analytical energy model estimates cost across four categories:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Analog MAC}: $E_{\text{MAC}} = N_{\text{ops}} \cdot E_{\text{cell}}$, which sets $E_{\text{cell}}=100$~fJ per differential-pair MAC in the default profiler.
    \item \textbf{Data Conversion}: ADC energy is charged per output element and DAC energy per input element, with behavioral DNL/INL modeling. The default implementation uses $E_{\text{ADC},8b}=25$~fJ, $E_{\text{DAC},8b}=30$~fJ, and $t_{\text{ADC},8b}=100$~ns for a SAR-like 8-bit readout proxy.
    \item \textbf{Digital Operations}: Fixed-point costs for Softmax, LayerNorm, and scaling.
    \item \textbf{Memory Traffic}: SRAM read/write energy for weight programming and intermediate activation buffering.
\end{enumerate}
The reported total is accumulated as
\[
E_{\mathrm{total}}
=
E_{\mathrm{MAC}}
+
E_{\mathrm{ADC}}
+
E_{\mathrm{DAC}}
+
E_{\mathrm{digital}}
+
E_{\mathrm{buffer}},
\]
with
\[
E_{\mathrm{ADC}} = N_{\mathrm{ADC}}E_{\mathrm{ADC},8b},
\qquad
E_{\mathrm{DAC}} = N_{\mathrm{DAC}}E_{\mathrm{DAC},8b},
\]
and the digital/buffer terms evaluated from operator and access counts. These constants are literature-derived proxy values for a first-order edge-node model, not measured circuit values, and any reported energy ratios are contingent on these assumptions. Interconnect overhead is behaviorally absorbed into the memory-access terms.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Analysis of Frontend Compensation}
\label{note:frontend-theory}

\paragraph{Distinction from ISP gamma correction.}
The inverse-gamma preprocessor $P_{\text{in}} = X^{1/\gamma_{\text{phys}}}$ is superficially similar to gamma correction in digital image processing (ISP), but the two serve different physical purposes and operate in different noise regimes. In ISP, gamma encoding ($\gamma_{\text{ISP}} \approx 2.2$) compresses high-intensity pixel values to match the nonlinear response of the human visual system, while its inverse (gamma decoding) expands the signal before display. The goal is perceptual uniformity, not physical linearization, and the noise model is typically signal-independent or post-quantization. The transformation is applied to already-digitized pixels.

In the organic phototransistor front end modeled here, the nonlinearity is a physical transduction property: photocurrent follows $I_{\text{photo}} \propto P^{\gamma_{\text{phys}}}$ with $\gamma_{\text{phys}}$ typically between 0.5 and 2.0. The inverse-gamma preprocessor $P_{\text{in}} = X^{1/\gamma_{\text{phys}}}$ is applied \emph{before} the physical transduction step, not after sensor readout. Consequently, shot noise $\varepsilon_{\text{shot}}$ is injected \emph{after} the compensation and scales with the compensated photocurrent. This ordering is critical: ISP gamma decoding operates on already-noisy quantized pixels, whereas the front-end compensation reshapes the signal that subsequently acquires noise. The result is a regime-dependent trade-off that has no analogue in ISP: for $\gamma_{\text{phys}} > 1$, compensation linearizes the signal and improves accuracy; for $\gamma_{\text{phys}} < 1$, it amplifies shot-noise variance across the entire intensity range, yielding marginal or negative benefit (Table~\ref{tab:supp-frontend-gamma-scan}). This signal-to-noise coupling is intrinsic to the phototransistor transduction chain and is captured explicitly by the compensated photocurrent model introduced in the main text. Consequently, the compensation exponent is chosen to linearize a physical transduction law, not to match a perceptual response curve.

Because the preprocessor is parameterized by $\gamma_{\text{phys}}$ and exposed through the same JSON device profile as conductance window, retention, and variability, it functions as a \emph{replaceable system-level design primitive} rather than as a fixed preprocessing step.

\paragraph{Why the transformer amplifies front-end distortion.}
The difference in front-end sensitivity between ViT and CNN can be understood from the way each architecture processes perturbed inputs. In a CNN, the initial convolutional layers apply local receptive-field operations. A sublinear photoresponse ($\gamma_{\text{phys}} > 1$) compresses bright-region contrast, but because each output pixel depends only on a small spatial neighborhood, the distortion remains localized in early feature maps. Subsequent pooling and strided convolutions further average these local perturbations, and the piecewise-linear ReLU activations limit the propagation of intensity-dependent scaling errors. The net effect is that CNNs tolerate moderate input nonlinearities because spatial averaging and local nonlinearity act as implicit regularizers.

In a ViT, by contrast, the input is partitioned into patches and linearly projected into token embeddings. A sublinear photoresponse alters the relative intensities \emph{within} each patch before the embedding projection sees the data. The critical difference arises in self-attention. The attention map is computed as $A = \text{softmax}(QK^{\top}/\sqrt{d_k})$, where $Q$ and $K$ are linear projections of the token embeddings. If the photoresponse nonlinearity changes the intensity distribution within a patch, the resulting token embedding shifts, and the dot-product similarity changes in a \emph{nonlinear} way because the embedding was learned on clean data. The softmax then \emph{exponentiates} these similarity changes: a small perturbation in one token can redistribute attention weights across the entire image. Because attention is global, there is no spatial averaging to dampen the perturbation before classification.

The experimental evidence supports this mechanism. Under the raw photoresponse ($\gamma_{\text{phys}}=2.0$ without compensation), ResNet-18 (R4) degrades from 90.37\% to 84.04\% ($-$6.3 pp). The transformer architecture is structurally more exposed to front-end distortion because global attention lacks the spatial averaging present in CNNs; this mechanism-level expectation is consistent with the ResNet-18 drop and with the stronger compensation sensitivity observed along the Tiny-ViT V6 pathway. After inverse-gamma compensation, both architectures recover, but the ViT recovery remains more sensitive to the compensation exponent because residual noise variance also propagates through the global attention map.

\subsubsection{Optimal Compensation Exponent}
\label{note:optimal-gamma}

The physical inverse $\gamma_c = 1/\gamma_{\text{phys}}$ exactly linearizes the photoresponse in the noiseless limit, but it is not necessarily optimal when shot noise is present. We derive the mean-squared-error-optimal compensation exponent for a uniform input distribution.

The effective signal deviation from the ideal linear response $I_{\text{ideal}} = \alpha X + I_{\text{dark}}$ is
\begin{equation}
    \Delta(X; \gamma_c) = \alpha \left(X^{\gamma_c \gamma_{\text{phys}}} - X\right) + \varepsilon_{\text{shot}},
\end{equation}
with $\text{Var}[\varepsilon_{\text{shot}}] = \sigma^2 (\alpha X^{\gamma_c \gamma_{\text{phys}}} + I_{\text{dark}})$. The expected MSE is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\gamma_c) = \alpha^2 \int_0^1 \!\left(X^{\gamma_c \gamma_{\text{phys}}} - X\right)^2 dX + \sigma^2 \int_0^1 \!\left(\alpha X^{\gamma_c \gamma_{\text{phys}}} + I_{\text{dark}}\right) dX.
\end{equation}

Setting $\beta = \gamma_c \gamma_{\text{phys}}$ and evaluating the integrals gives
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\beta) = \alpha^2\!\left[\frac{1}{2\beta+1} - \frac{2}{\beta+2} + \frac{1}{3}\right] + \sigma^2\!\left[\frac{\alpha}{\beta+1} + I_{\text{dark}}\right].
\end{equation}

Differentiating at $\beta = 1$ (the physical inverse):
\begin{equation}
    \left.\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\beta}\right|_{\beta=1} = -\frac{\sigma^2 \alpha}{4} < 0 \quad (\text{for } I_{\text{dark}} \approx 0).
\end{equation}

Because the derivative is negative, the MSE can be reduced by increasing $\beta$; i.e., the optimal compensation exponent is \textbf{milder} than the physical inverse when shot noise is present. The deviation is small for typical device parameters ($\sigma^2 \ll \alpha$), which explains why the physical inverse remains an excellent practical approximation. We note that this derivation minimizes pixel-level mean-squared error rather than task-level classification loss; the learnable-compensation experiment (E3) therefore tests whether end-to-end accuracy optimization recovers a deviation consistent with the MSE prediction.

\subsection{Comparison with Inorganic RRAM Baselines}
\label{subsec:inorganic-comparison}

To contextualize the specialized framework for organic optoelectronic devices, we contrast our methodology with mature inorganic simulations (e.g., RRAM arrays modeled via NeuroSim, CrossSim, or AIHWKIT) \citep{peng2020dnnneurosim,crosssim2024,rasch2021aihwkit}. Inorganic devices typically exhibit faster write speeds, larger on/off ratios, and primarily electrical programming dynamics. As a methodological consistency check in a shared regime, we evaluated an existing ResNet-18/CIFAR-10 model over the full 10k-sample test set under matched 4-bit/noise assumptions: the digital baseline reached \(95.46\%\), while an AIHWKIT analog-tile baseline reached \(90.08\pm0.21\%\) over 10 runs. We use this result only to verify numerical consistency between the present simulator and a mature inorganic toolkit under one matched regime; it does not establish physical validation for retention, photoresponse, or organic nonlinear writing. Thus, rather than claiming to outperform mature RRAM simulators on digital benchmarks, our framework serves as a complementary tool tailored to expose the system-level consequences of organic-specific physical non-idealities, such as shot noise amplification from inverse-gamma front ends.

\subsection{Automated Profile Fitting Pipeline}

For later measured-device studies, we also provide an automated fitting utility that ingests four raw-data categories:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pulsed Programming Curves}: Fits the conductance range ($G_{\min}, G_{\max}$) and nonlinearity coefficients ($NL_{LTP}, NL_{LTD}$) using a behavioral exponential model.
    \item \textbf{Cycle-to-Cycle (C2C) Statistics}: Extracts the $\sigma_{\mathrm{C2C}}$ noise parameter from repeated write-verify measurements at identical target states.
    \item \textbf{Device-to-Device (D2D) Mismatch}: Calculates the $\sigma_{\mathrm{D2D}}$ parameter from conductance distributions across multiple physical devices in an array.
    \item \textbf{Retention Decay}: Fits the double-exponential decay constants ($\tau_1, \tau_2, A_0$) from long-term conductance-vs-time measurements.
\end{enumerate}

The output is a standardized device-profile specification that can be used directly in the inference and HAT workflow. In the present manuscript, this fitter is included as supporting infrastructure for future measured-device calibration rather than as a core source of the reported headline results.

\paragraph{Supplementary Note~S1. Cross-framework subset-evaluation protocol.}
Because a single noisy evaluation on the full CIFAR-10 test set would extrapolate to roughly 15~h per condition for CrossSim (5,361~s per 1,000-image run) versus $\sim$1~s per 1,000-image run for the native framework, the shared-regime sanity check was performed on a deterministic 1,000-image slice of the canonical CIFAR-10 test ordering (the first 1,000 images; no RNG seed was set). This slice yields an approximately balanced class distribution (86--112 images per class). The clean baseline was evaluated once per framework; the noise-injection settings were evaluated with three independent Monte Carlo draws. For the single-run clean baseline, the Wilson 95\% confidence intervals on the subset proportion are [83.9\%, 88.2\%] for our framework and [81.3\%, 85.9\%] for CrossSim. For the three-run noise condition, the t-distribution 95\% CIs (df = 2, $t_{0.975} = 4.303$) are [79.9\%, 83.3\%] for our framework and [59.1\%, 75.3\%] for CrossSim. The large CrossSim run-to-run spread ($\sigma_{\text{pop}} = 2.67$\%) reflects the sensitivity of its generic programming-error/read-noise mapping to the particular stochastic sample. Independently of this subset check, the canonical fresh-instance collapse of standard HAT was re-evaluated under FP32 inference with autocast disabled and remained exactly $10.00\pm0.00$\% across 10 fresh D2D instances with 5 Monte Carlo evaluations per instance, confirming that the collapsed single-class predictor is a deterministic training outcome rather than a numerical artifact (\texttt{fresh\_instance\_eval\_v4\_standard\_noamp.json}).
