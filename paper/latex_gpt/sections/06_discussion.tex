\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

% =============================================================================
\subsection{Diagnosis: hardware-instance overfitting as the leading tested failure mode}
\label{subsec:diagnosis}

Once hardware-aware training (HAT) and scale recovery are available, standard cycle-to-cycle (C2C) and device-to-device (D2D) variability do not dominate the error budget. Three constraints dominate instead.

First, readout precision remains a hard gate: below 6 bits, transformer inference degrades substantially. Second, the leading tested training failure is hardware-instance overfitting. Standard HAT fits one fixed D2D realization and collapses to 10.00\% on fresh instances, whereas the mismatch-distributed objective in Eq.~\ref{eq:hat-ensemble-distribution} raises fresh-instance accuracy to $86.16\pm0.19\%$ across three seeds. Third, once this generalization problem is controlled, realistic PCM simulation exposes a separate precision-retention frontier rather than a single best compression point.

This ordering matters for design. The paper's main claim is not that one training recipe solves all device physics; it is that cross-instance robustness and physical retention should be solved in sequence. A residual gap to the 98.06\% digital baseline remains, and capacity-aware Ensemble HAT schedules are left as an open optimization problem.

% =============================================================================
\subsection{Treatment: Ensemble HAT mitigation across scenarios}
\label{subsec:treatment}

Ensemble HAT should be used when the observed failure mode is fresh-instance transfer under a low-precision analog-noise model. In that regime, epoch-level D2D resampling converts a 4-bit collapse into near-8-bit fresh-instance accuracy. It should not be read as a complete device-physics solution. Under the tested PCM UnitCell regime, 4-bit remains trainable but is drift-limited, whereas 6-bit and 8-bit are effectively drift-flat over the tested 24-hour window.

% =============================================================================
\subsection{Mechanism: theory and empirical landscape}
\label{subsec:mechanism}

Supplementary Note S-Theory gives the formal view: per-epoch resampling is, to second order, a weighted gradient-$L_2$ regularizer and a stochastic sharpness penalty along the device-mismatch direction. The PAC-Bayes argument explains why fresh-instance accuracy should track the training-distribution average when the training distribution spans the deployment mismatch.

The empirical diagnostics agree with this view. Loss interpolation from the training D2D mask toward fresh masks shows a $\sim$76~pp Standard-to-Ensemble gap, and the extrapolated $3\times$ mismatch test preserves a 17.5~pp advantage for Ensemble HAT. Hessian spectra add an important caution: Standard HAT can appear flatter at its own training mask, but that flatness is mask-specific. The relevant robustness metric is therefore D2D-directional sensitivity, not global curvature.

\subsection{Implications: design rules for deployment}
\label{subsec:implications}

Figure~\ref{fig:paper1-decision-map} gives the deployment order used by this work: diagnose fresh-instance collapse, train with a mismatch-distributed objective, then select the physical precision from the retention budget. In the tested PCM UnitCell setting, 4-bit is attractive for compression but drift-limited, while 6-bit is the safest observed midpoint. Detailed ADC/D2D envelope and front-end compensation checks are retained in the Supplementary Information rather than elevated to additional main-text claims.

\subsection{Comparison to established analog HAT primitives}
\label{subsec:aihwkit-comparison}

AIHWKit \citep{rasch2021aihwkit} per-batch noise injection is already strong at 8-bit ($87.28\pm0.13\%$ fresh-instance), but it collapses at 4-bit ($14.64\pm0.11\%$). Ensemble HAT maintains $86.16\pm0.19\%$ at 4-bit. Its advantage is therefore specific to the operating point where per-batch noise injection alone does not overcome mismatch variability. The PCM results then add a second constraint: all tested precisions train, but only 6-bit and 8-bit remain drift-flat over the tested window.

\subsection{Limitations and outlook}
\label{subsec:limitations}

\paragraph{Scope.} The conclusions are bounded by the present behavioral simulation stack. Device profiles are calibrated from literature values or a single fabrication batch, so the numbers do not claim cross-batch, temperature, aging, or circuit-layout coverage. We model quantization, D2D/C2C variability, ADC precision, write nonlinearity, and retention, but defer parasitic IR drop, sneak paths, and pulse-faithful programming to follow-on hardware-calibrated studies.

\paragraph{Energy and scale.} The energy estimates are first-order analytical projections from literature-proxy constants ($E_{\text{cell}}=100$~fJ, $E_{\text{ADC},8b}=25$~fJ, $E_{\text{DAC},8b}=30$~fJ; Section~\ref{subsec:sensitivity-energy}). They rank deployment risks rather than predict silicon performance. The current ablation matrix also prioritizes non-ideality coverage over dataset breadth; cross-architecture TinyImageNet sweeps are treated as validation, not as main-text claims.

\paragraph{Outlook.} The next tests are straightforward: replace UnitCell presets with measured-array statistics, verify whether the 6-bit midpoint persists, and evaluate memory-bound inference components such as analog KV-cache with explicit train/eval noise separation. These extensions do not alter the present conclusion: algorithmic cross-instance resampling and physical precision-retention selection are separate deployment decisions.
