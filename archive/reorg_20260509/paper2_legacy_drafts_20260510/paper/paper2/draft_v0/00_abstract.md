# Abstract

Analog compute-in-memory (CIM) promises orders-of-magnitude energy efficiency for edge vision by executing matrix-vector multiplications directly within memory arrays. Yet device non-idealities—particularly severe write nonlinearity and fixed hardware-instance mismatch—routinely collapse model accuracy far below deployment thresholds. Prior work has catalogued what works under moderate stress: ensemble hardware-aware training recovers strong fresh-instance accuracy, provided the write nonlinearity remains mild. What remains unknown is whether any training or architectural mitigation can recover comparable accuracy once the nonlinearity becomes severe. Here we adopt a falsification stance and systematically test three independent candidate remedies—MLP-only linearization, full linearization, and joint MLP-linear training coupled with epoch-level device-instance resampling—within the compute-ViT behavioral simulation framework. All three strategies recover source-domain accuracy, but fresh-instance evaluation across ten unseen hardware realizations converges on the same approximate 30 % ceiling under severe write nonlinearity (NL = 2.0). We interpret this convergence as evidence that severe nonlinearity imposes a structural generalization barrier in the attention pathway, one that first-order behavioral surrogates cannot train through regardless of architectural partitioning or distributional resampling. We close by proposing the CX-J1b/c/d diagnostic protocol to guide future work on higher-fidelity nonlinearity models.
