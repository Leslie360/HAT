# 1. Introduction

Compute-in-memory (CIM) and processing-in-memory (PIM) architectures are increasingly investigated as a response to the energy cost of moving weights and activations between memory and arithmetic units in edge inference systems \citep{horowitz2014computing,peng2020dnnneurosim}. While this motivation is often introduced through the broader memory-wall discussion around modern AI workloads, the practical setting addressed in this work is not large-language-model serving but low-power visual inference at the edge. In that regime, the key question is whether emerging organic optoelectronic synaptic devices can support useful image classification accuracy under realistic non-idealities while still preserving the energy advantage promised by analog vector-matrix multiplication.

Organic optoelectronic and electrochemical synaptic devices are attractive because they offer multi-level conductance tuning, optical sensitivity, and ultra-low static power, all of which are relevant to near-sensor computing \citep{xu2025emerging,guo2024organic}. However, most device-centric studies stop at idealized network-level validation. The dominant evaluation pattern remains small-scale inference under nominal conductance values, often without explicit cycle-to-cycle (C2C) variability, device-to-device (D2D) mismatch, analog-to-digital conversion constraints, or retention drift \citep{zeng2023organicmemristor,jung2024organicfilaments}. As a result, the literature still lacks a reproducible software stack that bridges device physics, crossbar mapping, and hardware-aware training for modern visual backbones.

This gap creates a practical dilemma for cross-disciplinary teams. A materials group can often measure conductance windows, LTP/LTD trajectories, photoresponse curves, and retention decay long before it can report a complete task-level AI benchmark. Conversely, a system or algorithm group needs exactly those device statistics to predict whether a given hardware stack will fail because of insufficient state resolution, instance mismatch, converter precision, or temporal drift. A useful simulation framework must therefore do more than inject abstract Gaussian noise: it must provide an interpretable path from partial device measurements to model-level accuracy, energy, and robustness trends, so that materials optimization can be guided by system-level consequences even before the final device dataset is fully locked.

This gap is especially visible for organic-device simulation. To our knowledge, the most complete prior Monte Carlo study with explicit variability modeling is the early organic memristor work of Alibart et al. on MNIST-scale perceptrons \citep{alibart2016physical}. Alibart et al. employed 500 Monte Carlo trials with a single-layer perceptron on MNIST, whereas the present work injects stochastic device noise directly into the training loop of multi-million-parameter vision backbones on CIFAR-10 and therefore realizes implicit Monte Carlo sampling through per-forward C2C resampling. More recent organic synapse papers typically report encouraging task accuracy, yet many evaluate under idealized device assumptions and do not expose a system-level robustness pipeline that can be reproduced in a modern deep learning framework. In parallel, general CIM simulators such as DNN+NeuroSim and MemTorch have matured for inorganic resistive memories, but they do not directly establish how organic optoelectronic device assumptions propagate through hybrid analog/digital deployment choices for convolutional and transformer vision models \citep{peng2020dnnneurosim,memtorch}.

This paper addresses that gap through a full-stack simulation workflow centered on organic optoelectronic synaptic transistors for edge vision inference. We start from crossbar-aware mapping rules for Tiny-ViT-5M, distinguish which operators should remain in analog or digital domains, and then inject quantization, C2C/D2D variability, ADC distortion, and retention effects directly into the forward path used for training and evaluation. The workflow is first validated on ResNet-18, extended to ConvNeXt-Tiny as a high-capacity convolutional baseline, and then transferred to Tiny-ViT as the target hybrid transformer architecture. In addition, we study a front-end inverse-gamma compensation mechanism motivated by the sub-linear photocurrent response of organic phototransistors, while explicitly preserving the associated signal-to-noise trade-off instead of presenting compensation as a universally beneficial transformation.

The present study makes four contributions. First, it provides, to the best of our knowledge, the first PyTorch-based hardware-aware training pipeline for system-level simulation of organic optoelectronic synaptic devices under joint quantization and variability. Second, it extends organic-device robustness evaluation from simple perceptrons to contemporary vision backbones, including a Vision Transformer family model, thereby testing whether transformer-style dense projections remain deployable under realistic device constraints. Third, it integrates multiple non-idealities in one stack, including differential-pair conductance mapping, discrete conductance states, C2C and D2D variability, ADC distortion, and double-exponential retention decay. Fourth, it introduces and critically evaluates an inverse-gamma physical front-end compensation strategy, showing that any benefit in low-signal regions must be interpreted alongside bright-region noise amplification rather than as a free robustness gain.
