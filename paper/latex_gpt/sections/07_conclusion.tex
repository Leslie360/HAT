\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a first-order behavioral simulation framework for organic optoelectronic compute-in-memory inference and validated it across two complementary testbeds: ConvNeXt-Tiny trained from scratch and Tiny-ViT-5M fine-tuned from a pre-trained foundation model. The framework connects differential conductance mapping, quantization, variability, retention, ADC limits, optical front-end distortion, and profile-driven device substitution within one reproducible evaluation stack, thereby narrowing the gap between device-level characterization and task-level vision performance.

Three conclusions emerge most clearly. First, the impact of analog non-idealities is strongly regime-dependent. Under the canonical uniform-noise model, quantization and nominal stochastic noise are not the dominant accuracy bottlenecks, especially on CIFAR-10. Second, that apparent robustness does not generalize universally: task complexity, fresh hardware instances, proportional state-dependent noise, and nonlinear write dynamics all expose much sharper failure modes, with transformer-based deployments consistently more fragile than CNN baselines under the richer physics. Third, hardware-aware training remains valuable, but its benefit depends on both the task and the physical regime. It recovers large fractions of the CIFAR-100 degradation and can relearn proportional-noise robustness, yet it does not solve severe nonlinear-write distortion and it reaches a clear data-volume floor on Flowers-102.

The main contribution of this work is therefore not a claim of full physical predictiveness, but the establishment of a structured methodology for evaluating the sensitivity of neural network performance to device-level parameters, enabling materials researchers to prioritize optimization targets before committing to fabrication. By making the modeling assumptions explicit, by separating canonical results from harder stress-test regimes, and by centering the software stack on replaceable device profiles, the present framework offers a practical path for future materials studies: literature priors can be used for development today, and measured device parameters can later be inserted without changing the surrounding evaluation logic. In that sense, the framework is intended less as a final answer about one device and more as a transparent bridge between partial materials characterization and system-level design decisions.
