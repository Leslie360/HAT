# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Organic Neuromorphic and Optoelectronic Devices

Organic synaptic and memristive devices have been explored as a route toward flexible, low-power neuromorphic hardware, with reported advantages in optical sensitivity, conductance programmability, and heterogeneous material integration \citep{xu2025emerging}. Prior work has demonstrated multi-level conductance updates, long retention tails, and proof-of-concept learning demonstrations in material systems such as Fe-TBP, PEDOT:PSS, TIPS-Pen, and related organic or hybrid optoelectronic stacks \citep{alibart2016physical,jung2024organicfilaments,guo2024organic}. These studies are important because they motivate physically plausible parameter ranges for conductance states, dynamic range, and retention behavior.

At the same time, most organic-device papers remain device-centric. Network validation is frequently limited to idealized weight mappings or small benchmarks, and variability is often either excluded or summarized qualitatively. The most relevant methodological precursor is the Monte Carlo study of Alibart et al., which explicitly sampled device parameters and evaluated a simple perceptron under repeated trials \citep{alibart2016physical}. Our work follows that spirit of variability-aware simulation, but moves to modern deep learning software, larger CIFAR-scale tasks, and substantially more expressive backbones.

## 2.2 CIM Simulation Frameworks

System-level CIM simulators such as DNN+NeuroSim have established a strong template for linking device assumptions, peripheral-circuit costs, and neural-network accuracy \citep{peng2020dnnneurosim}. Likewise, MemTorch demonstrated how memristive non-idealities can be embedded into PyTorch workflows for end-to-end evaluation \citep{memtorch}, while IBM's AIHWKIT exposed analog hardware-aware training and inference simulation in a practical mixed-signal software stack \citep{rasch2021aihwkit}. These frameworks are highly influential, but they primarily target inorganic resistive memories and do not directly answer the deployment questions raised by organic optoelectronic devices, especially when the target architecture includes mixed convolutional and transformer operators.

The distinction is substantive rather than cosmetic. DNN+NeuroSim is strongest when the goal is calibrated co-estimation of array and peripheral cost under established electrical NVM assumptions, but it does not natively model optically driven transduction or a profile-substitution workflow centered on partial organic-device characterization. MemTorch and AIHWKIT both provide valuable analog-tile abstractions inside PyTorch, yet neither is organized around explicit photoresponse-aware frontends, literature-to-profile parameter provenance, or the hybrid operator partition required for Tiny-ViT deployment. We therefore position the present framework as a complementary organic-CIM methodology rather than as a generic replacement for mature inorganic simulators.

Our workflow is therefore complementary rather than competitive. We reuse the general simulator logic of layer replacement, forward-path perturbation, and peripheral-aware energy accounting, but specialize the parameterization to organic-device operating regimes and to a hybrid mapping policy that is tuned for Tiny-ViT. This specialization matters because the crossbar-worthy operators in transformer backbones are not identical to those in conventional CNNs, and because the resulting analog fraction depends strongly on how one treats patch embedding, dense projections, depthwise convolutions, normalization, and attention specials. Relative to NeuroSim, MemTorch, and AIHWKIT, the present framework differs in three respects: it targets organic optoelectronic devices with explicit photoresponse modeling, it provides a profile-driven substitution interface for arbitrary device technologies, and it jointly evaluates inference accuracy, retention, and energy under a unified protocol across CNN and transformer backbones.

## 2.3 Hardware-Aware Training and Fault Tolerance

Quantization-aware training and hardware-aware training have long shown that accuracy under deployment non-idealities can be recovered when these effects are exposed during optimization \citep{jacob2018quantization,bengio2013estimating}. In non-volatile-memory accelerators, the same principle appears under names such as fault-aware training, noise-aware training, and device-aware training, usually with gains ranging from several to tens of percentage points depending on the severity of the mismatch between training-time and inference-time hardware conditions.

This literature provides the conceptual basis for our A2 and A3 experiments. However, existing reports are dominated by inorganic devices and by CNN-oriented architectures. What remains underexplored is whether the same recovery pattern survives in a hybrid transformer deployment where the majority of parameters reside in analog-mapped dense projections, but softmax, normalization, and local depthwise operators remain digital. That gap motivates our direct comparison between standard training, D2D-only adaptation, and full hardware-aware training.

## 2.4 ViT Mapping and Hybrid Analog/Digital Scheduling

Recent PIM literature has emphasized that dense linear operators are efficient crossbar targets, whereas depthwise or highly irregular operators often suffer from poor utilization \citep{wu2023bwq,wang2024epim,ge2024allspark}. Studies such as BWQ and EPIM argue that output-unit constraints and array tiling can drastically reduce effective parallelism for depthwise convolutions, while ViT-style dense projections frequently retain high utilization. This observation underlies our mapping policy: query-key-value projections, output projections, multilayer-perceptron layers, and patch-embedding convolutions are treated as analog candidates, whereas depthwise convolutions, normalization, activation functions, and dynamic attention products remain digital.

The present work differs from prior ViT-on-PIM papers in two ways. First, our target device family is organic optoelectronic rather than mature inorganic resistive memory. Second, we explicitly co-model variability, quantization, ADC effects, and retention in the same framework instead of reporting only mapping utilization or ideal analog acceleration. We therefore position the work at the intersection of device-aware CIM simulation and architecture-aware mapping for edge vision transformers.
