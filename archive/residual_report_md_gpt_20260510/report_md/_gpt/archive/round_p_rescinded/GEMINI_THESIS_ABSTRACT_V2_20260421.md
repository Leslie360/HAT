# Thesis Abstract — Hardware-Instance Overfitting and Robust Deployment in Organic Optoelectronic CIM

This dissertation began with a disquieting observation: a vision model trained on one organic optoelectronic array could score eighty-eight percent, yet collapse to ten percent the moment it met a fresh hardware instance. That gap became the initiating discovery of a systematic falsification program rather than a single success story. Standard hardware-aware training fails on fresh instances because the optimizer does not learn robust representations; it memorizes the specific noise fingerprint of the training-time device, a phenomenon this thesis identifies and characterizes as hardware-instance overfitting. The first recovery is Ensemble HAT, which treats each training forward pass as a Monte Carlo draw and marginalizes over instance variability rather than encoding it. Under mild nonlinearity, ensemble resampling restores eighty-six percent with bounded variance, but beneath this headline lies a deeper and more consequential structure. When nonlinearity grows severe, the same mitigation strategies plateau near thirty percent, revealing a hard ceiling that simulation can predict but training cannot breach. This thesis therefore constructs a failure-mode atlas spanning representational collapse, attention-path fragility, severe nonlinearity bottlenecks, correlated device-to-device mismatch, and retention drift, and translates that atlas into a deployment envelope that tells engineers when an analog chip is ready and when it is not. The three core contributions are a reusable simulation framework that enables distributional reasoning, the discovery of hardware-instance overfitting together with a taxonomy of Hardware-Aware Training regimes, and rigorous characterization of deployment limits including the negative results that define them. Simulation can bound the risk, but only measured-device calibration can close it. Together, these contributions replace single-array benchmarking with fleet-level reasoning and establish that analog CIM must be evaluated not by its best-case headline, but by the worst-case instance it is willing to ship.
