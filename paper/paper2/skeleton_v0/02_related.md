# §2 Related Work

## 2.1 CIM Simulators and Training Frameworks

The analog-CIM research ecosystem is anchored by a family of behavioral simulators that translate device non-idealities into task-level accuracy degradation. IBM's AIHWKIT provides differentiable conductance models with programmable noise sources, enabling gradient-based hardware-aware training for resistive crossbars. NeuroSim and MNSIM operate at the circuit–architecture interface, modeling array-level parasitics, ADC quantization, and sneak-path leakage with technology-calibrated parameters. CrossSim extends this stack with profile-driven device substitution, allowing researchers to swap literature-derived organic RRAM parameters into a common simulation backbone. Our own compute-ViT framework builds on these foundations by adding vision-transformer-specific mapping rules, ensemble training protocols, and fresh-instance evaluation that tests generalization across unseen hardware realizations rather than merely fitting a fixed noise model.

A persistent limitation across these frameworks is the treatment of device noise as statistically homogeneous. Most simulators inject i.i.d. Gaussian perturbations to conductances or weights, which captures C2C variability but obscures the spatially structured nature of D2D mismatch maps. In fabricated arrays, mismatch is correlated across the wafer, with smooth spatial gradients and die-level offsets that create coherent distortion patterns. Ensemble HAT addresses this by resampling full spatial mismatch maps rather than independent per-cell noise, but the broader simulator landscape has been slow to adopt structured mismatch modeling. This paper assumes spatially structured D2D fields and treats instance overfitting as a first-class failure mode.

## 2.2 Hardware-Aware Training for CIM

Hardware-aware training (HAT) for analog CIM follows the quantization-aware training paradigm: device noise is injected into the forward pass during training so that the optimizer learns weights that are robust to the expected deployment perturbation. Standard HAT fixes a single D2D mismatch realization for the entire training run. This approach is computationally cheap but invites hardware-instance overfitting: the model learns to compensate for one specific spatial distortion and collapses when tested on a fresh array.

Variation-aware training extends standard HAT by sampling multiple mismatch realizations within a batch or epoch, a strategy analogous to domain randomization in sim-to-real robotics. Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) and related flat-minimum seekers have been proposed as generic robustness enhancers, though their parameter-space isotropy assumptions do not map cleanly onto the structured mismatch manifolds of analog arrays. Ensemble HAT, which resamples the full D2D map at every epoch, recovers strong fresh-instance accuracy under moderate nonlinearity ($\text{NL}=1.0$), establishing that instance overfitting is solvable when the surrogate remains locally valid. What remains unknown—and what this paper addresses—is whether any HAT variant survives once the nonlinearity invalidates the local surrogate approximation.

## 2.3 Analog Nonlinearity Models

Conductance programming in analog memories is inherently nonlinear. The programmed state depends on the history of pulses, the current conductance, and device-specific variability. The modeling literature spans two poles: empirical surrogates and physics-based compact models.

Empirical surrogates dominate the open-source training ecosystem because they are differentiable and cheap to evaluate. The first-order gradient-scaling surrogate, which models effective conductance as a deterministic power law and back-propagates through a straight-through estimator (STE), is the de facto standard. It is accurate when the relative conductance deviation is small ($\text{NL} \approx 1.0$) but becomes a poor approximation as the exponent grows. Piecewise-linear and spline surrogates offer local improvements but do not address the fundamental mismatch between the training loss landscape and the physical inference loss landscape under severe curvature.

Physics-based compact models capture the full programming dynamics— filament formation, charge trapping, thermal activation—but are typically too expensive to embed in the training loop. The gap between cheap first-order surrogates and expensive physics models is a central tension in analog-AI research. This paper stays within the first-order surrogate family to establish its boundary, then derives a second-order Taylor-corrected STE as a falsification probe to test whether the barrier is a truncation artifact or a deeper structural property.

## 2.4 Vision Transformers on Analog Hardware

The migration of ViTs to analog substrates is recent and precarious. Early work mapped fully connected and convolutional layers to crossbar arrays with promising energy–accuracy trade-offs, but attention blocks pose distinct challenges. The QKV projections are large linear layers that map cleanly to arrays, yet the attention score matrix involves a bilinear form ($QK^{\top}$) followed by an exponential normalization (softmax), creating sharp winner-take-all maps that are structurally sensitive to multiplicative weight perturbations. Prior work on ViT quantization and pruning has characterized the attention pathway as the accuracy bottleneck under aggressive compression; we extend this insight to the analog domain, where the bottleneck is not bit-width reduction but conductance nonlinearity.

No prior work systematically evaluates ViT attention blocks under severe write nonlinearity with fresh-instance validation. Existing analog-ViT studies report source-domain accuracy only, leaving the deployment transfer question open. This paper is the first to subject analog-mapped ViT attention to block-heterogeneous nonlinearity partitioning and fresh-instance generalization testing.

## 2.5 Negative Results, Falsification, and Structural Limits

The machine-learning literature has historically under-valued negative results, yet there is growing recognition that converging negative evidence is essential for scientific progress. The lottery-ticket hypothesis was sharpened by bounds showing when it fails; No Free Lunch theorems constrain what any learning algorithm can achieve without inductive bias. In the analog-CIM domain, negative results are particularly valuable because fabrication runs are expensive: knowing where *not* to invest effort prevents wasted resources.

Karl Popper's falsification principle provides the epistemic framework for this paper. A scientific claim earns its status by exposing itself to genuine risk of refutation. We treat the structural-limit hypothesis as falsifiable: if any of three independent mitigations breaks the $30\%$ ceiling, the hypothesis is refuted. The fact that all three converge on the same bound strengthens the claim rather than weakening it. This posture distinguishes our work from reports of isolated failed experiments: we design the failure pattern to be informative.

## 2.6 What We Borrow and What Is New

We borrow the compute-ViT simulation framework, the ensemble HAT protocol, the organic-RRAM device profiles, and the canonical fresh-instance evaluation pipeline ($10$ instances × $5$ MC passes) from prior work. What is new is the systematic block-heterogeneous linearization study under severe NL, the formal structural-limit hypothesis with its three theoretical pillars, the pre-registered CX-J diagnostic protocol, and the explicit falsification posture. We also contribute the first derivation of a second-order STE tailored to analog conductance programming, framed as a falsification probe rather than a claimed breakthrough.
