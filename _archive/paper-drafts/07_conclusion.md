# 7. Conclusion

We presented a first-order behavioral simulation framework for organic optoelectronic compute-in-memory inference whose main purpose is to solve a deployment-decision problem: given partial device characterization, which behaviors already look sufficient for modern edge-vision deployment, and which ones still dominate the failure risk? The framework connects differential conductance mapping, quantization, variability, retention, ADC limits, optical front-end distortion, and profile-driven device substitution within one reproducible evaluation stack, thereby narrowing the gap between device-level characterization and task-level vision performance.

Three conclusions emerge most clearly. First, the impact of analog non-idealities is strongly regime-dependent. Under the canonical uniform-noise model, quantization and nominal stochastic noise are not the dominant accuracy bottlenecks, especially on CIFAR-10. Second, that apparent robustness does not generalize automatically to deployment: task complexity, fresh hardware instances, proportional state-dependent noise, and nonlinear write dynamics all expose much sharper failure modes. The most important corrective result in the paper is therefore not same-instance HAT, but Ensemble HAT, which raises fresh-instance Tiny-ViT accuracy from 10.00% to 86.37 ± 1.54%. Third, hardware-aware retraining can recover some matched physical regimes, such as proportional noise at 97.37 ± 0.05%, but it does not solve everything. Severe nonlinear-write distortion remains stuck at 27.72 ± 0.82% for the present first-order approximation, and the Flowers-102 boundary remains a hypothesis-generating failure case rather than a proved causal story.

The main contribution of this work is therefore not a claim of full physical predictiveness, but the establishment of a structured methodology for evaluating the sensitivity of neural network performance to device-level parameters before full chip availability. By making the modeling assumptions explicit, by separating canonical results from harder stress-test regimes, and by centering the software stack on replaceable device profiles, the framework offers a practical path for future materials studies: literature priors can be used for development today, and measured device parameters can later be inserted without changing the surrounding evaluation logic. The Zhang et al. (2025) OPECT case study, which reaches 88.53% through a literature-derived profile, is the clearest evidence that this bridge is already usable in practice. Likewise, the reported energy gains should be interpreted as first-order, upper-bound-like profiler outputs under shared operation-count assumptions rather than as full-layout chip measurements.

The broader message is therefore targeted and bounded. We do not claim a chip-predictive emulator, nor do we claim that present organic-device assumptions already make all modern architectures equally deployable. Instead, we show that a transparent behavioral bridge can already answer the questions that matter most to device and system teams: which non-idealities are secondary, which ones dominate first, how much can retraining recover, and where the present engineering boundaries still remain. In that sense, the framework is intended less as a final answer about one device and more as a reusable decision tool for prioritizing the next round of materials and hardware optimization.
