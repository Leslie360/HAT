# 6. Discussion

## 6.1 What Actually Limits Organic-CIM Accuracy?

The combined ResNet, ConvNeXt, and Tiny-ViT analyses revise the intuitive failure hierarchy one might expect from an organic analog accelerator. Standard cycle-to-cycle (C2C) and device-to-device (D2D) variability are not the primary drivers of accuracy loss when paired with hardware-aware training (HAT) and proper scale recovery. Instead, the results point to three more fundamental bottlenecks.

First, **ADC resolution acts as a critical threshold within the present simulator configuration.** The 6-bit cliff observed in the Tiny-ViT sweep suggests that Transformer-based architectures have a rigid quantization floor below which the attention mechanism collapses. This is physically significant: while materials scientists can tune conductance states, the system-level benefit is still gated by the power and area cost of the readout converters.

Second, **hardware-instance overfitting** is a severe risk for analog foundation models. The collapse of V4 under fresh-instance transfer (10.00% accuracy) indicates that standard HAT can overfit a single D2D realization. Resampling D2D masks during training raises fresh-instance accuracy to 86.37 ± 1.54%, showing that multi-instance-aware training can mitigate the dominant transfer failure mode in the present setting. However, this improved transferability appears to come with a robustness--capacity trade-off: the Ensemble HAT checkpoint does not fully close the gap to the 98.06% digital baseline even under matched deployment conditions, so part of the remaining gap may reflect regularization from per-epoch D2D resampling rather than analog noise alone.

Third, **what we descriptively call the scale-masking effect provides a conditional rather than universal form of robustness.** The observation that V2 survives standard uniform noise at 97% accuracy is an algorithmic property of the canonical simulator configuration. By rescaling the analog output back to the digital range, the effective perturbation can fall below the 4-bit quantization step. This protection relies on accurate digital post-array gain calibration and does not survive under all physics extensions, as demonstrated by the proportional-noise stress tests.

## 6.2 Transformer Sensitivity to Non-Idealities

Our comparison of the dual testbeds—ConvNeXt (CNN) and Tiny-ViT (Transformer)—shows that the Transformer deployment is more fragile than the CNN deployment under the present physical stress tests. Because the two models serve complementary roles (ConvNeXt from scratch versus Tiny-ViT from pre-trained fine-tuning), this should be read as evidence of a strong architecture-and-training interaction rather than as an unconditional claim that all Transformers are less robust than all CNNs.

1. **State-Dependent Noise Sensitivity**: The sharp collapse of V4 under proportional noise (10.00% accuracy), contrasted with the relative stability of ConvNeXt under moderate non-linearities, indicates that the Transformer deployment depends heavily on the precision of every token projection. Because the attention map is a global softmax product, a large noise spike in a single high-conductance device can disrupt the entire spatial focus of the block.
2. **Physical Frontend Distortion**: The greater accuracy drop in V6 (Tiny-ViT with optical frontend) compared to ResNet R4 indicates that the Transformer deployment converts local pixel noise into sequence-wide attention scatter more readily. This suggests that materials-pure synaptic acceleration is insufficient; the precision of the input transduction layer is equally critical.
3. **Regime-Specific Recoverability**: The proportional-noise retraining result sharpens the comparison further. Tiny-ViT can recover strongly when trained and evaluated under the same proportional-noise law, yet the resulting checkpoint does not transfer back to the uniform-noise regime. This indicates that robustness in the present framework is highly distribution-matched rather than universally noise-invariant.

## 6.3 Task Complexity and Data Starvation

The multi-dataset results establish a fundamental relationship between task grain and analog robustness.

- **Complexity Scaling**: HAT recovery is most valuable on CIFAR-100, where decision boundaries are fine. On CIFAR-10, the problem is sufficiently easy that even noisy standard models remain functional.
- **The HAT Data-Floor Hypothesis**: Flowers-102 defines a failure boundary for the current HAT recipe: Tiny-ViT recovers only partially and ConvNeXt does not recover. Low effective data volume is a plausible explanation, but domain shift, hyperparameter mismatch, and architecture-specific effects remain viable alternatives. We therefore treat data starvation as a working hypothesis rather than as a settled causal claim.

## 6.4 The Analog Ceiling and Energy Efficiency

Despite the current first-order, upper-bound-like estimate of an 11.45x reduction in dense-projection energy, the overall efficiency of Transformer-CIM is limited by the **digital execution of dynamic attention**. That 11.45x figure is FP32-referenced and should be read as a profiler output under shared operation-count assumptions rather than as a matched chip-level measurement. Our energy profiling shows that digital operations still dominate the system-level cost, with 57.9% of total energy in some regimes. This creates an analog ceiling: as long as the QK^T product and Softmax remain in the von Neumann digital domain, the total system speedup will be sub-linear with respect to the analog MAC speedup. True materials-level impact will require either more efficient digital attention backends or fundamentally different mechanisms for accelerating dynamic attention-like operations.

## 6.5 Comparison with Inorganic RRAM Baselines

To contextualize the necessity of a specialized framework for organic optoelectronic devices, we contrast our methodology with mature inorganic simulations such as RRAM arrays modeled via NeuroSim, CrossSim, or AIHWKIT. Inorganic devices typically exhibit faster write speeds, larger on/off ratios, and primarily electrical programming dynamics. In contrast, the organic devices modeled here present a different Pareto frontier: they offer multi-modal optoelectronic tuning and excellent low-power analog behavior, but often suffer from slower temporal drift, pronounced state-dependent retention, and complex non-linear write asymmetry. As a bounded shared-regime sanity check, we evaluated an existing ResNet-18/CIFAR-10 checkpoint over the full 10k-sample test set under matched 4-bit/noise assumptions: the digital baseline reached 95.46%, while an AIHWKIT analog-tile proxy reached 90.08 ± 0.21% over 10 runs. We use this result only to show that the present simulator and a mature inorganic toolkit exhibit the same qualitative degradation trend under one matched regime; it does not establish full physics equivalence for retention, photoresponse, or organic nonlinear writing. Thus, rather than claiming to outperform mature RRAM simulators on digital benchmarks, our framework serves as a complementary tool tailored to expose the system-level consequences of organic-specific physical non-idealities, such as shot-noise amplification from inverse-gamma frontends.

## 6.6 Limitations

While the framework provides structured system-level insights, several physical idealizations must be explicitly acknowledged as limitations of the present behavioral model:

- **Reproducibility Boundary**: The released workflow uses explicit seeds and deterministic cuDNN settings in the main training and evaluation entry points, but we do not claim cross-platform bitwise identity across hardware, drivers, or mixed-precision kernels. The reproducibility target of this paper is therefore seeded execution-trace consistency through matched configs, logs, and checkpoint lineage.
- **First-Order Energy Model**: Array-to-digital interconnect energy is absorbed into the SRAM read/write cost terms. Dedicated routing overhead, including data marshaling between the analog output bus and the digital coprocessor, is not separately itemized. Using the current Tiny-ViT profiler numbers, adding extra routing overhead equal to 10%, 30%, or 50% of the analog-MAC budget would raise the hybrid estimate from 273.94 μJ to 282.52, 299.70, and 316.87 μJ, reducing the FP32-referenced gain from 11.45x to about 11.10x, 10.47x, and 9.90x, respectively. This does not remove the need for a routed circuit model, but it indicates that the qualitative advantage is not erased by moderate unmodeled interconnect cost.
- **Differential-Pair Symmetry**: The differential mapping assumes that positive and negative branches share the same nominal transfer characteristics apart from sampled stochastic perturbations. Systematic branch asymmetry from layout, contact resistance, or programming-history mismatch is therefore not isolated as a separate error source in the present study.
- **Hardware Array Non-Idealities**: Position-dependent IR drop along crossbar wordlines and bitlines, as well as sneak-path currents in passive arrays, are not modeled \citep{fastirdrop2025,iconniv2025,riam2025sneakpath}. These effects introduce systematic, input-pattern-dependent weight distortion distinct from stochastic C2C/D2D variability.
- **Non-Linear Writing**: Our implementation of `NL_LTP/NL_LTD` is a gradient-scaling approximation. In actual pulsed programming, the stochastic nature of ionic/electronic migration may further degrade the HAT recovery.
- **State-Dependent Drift**: While the framework now supports state-dependent retention models where high-conductance states decay faster, the canonical results reported here use uniform double-exponential decay. Our preliminary evaluation suggests robustness to this effect, but extreme drift asymmetry in emerging organic materials remains a potential deployment risk.
- **INL/DNL Complexity**: The framework includes support for conductance integral non-linearity (INL) via non-uniform lookup tables. However, the stochastic interaction between INL and dynamic programming noise in physical crossbars remains a second-order effect that is approximated rather than pulse-simulated.
- **Temperature Effects**: The current framework does not model temperature-dependent mobility or threshold-voltage shifts \citep{fuller2020tempresilient,guo2024hightemp}. These field-relevant effects for organic semiconductors remain a priority extension.
- **Optical Non-Uniformity**: Light-intensity non-uniformity across the array aperture and optical write crosstalk between adjacent synaptic elements are not modeled \citep{visionarch2023crosstalk,amspa2024insensor}. For optoelectronic synaptic transistors where programming is optically driven, these effects may introduce spatially correlated weight errors distinct from the stochastic D2D variability considered here.

## 6.7 Future Directions

The most important next step is to close the loop between laboratory characterization and the simulator. At present, the framework already accepts calibrated JSON device profiles, but the conversion from raw measurements to fitted profile parameters is still manual. A deployment-oriented materials workflow should ingest multilevel programming curves, repeated-write statistics, retention traces, and photoresponse measurements and convert them automatically into G_min/G_max, effective state count, D2D/C2C variability, retention constants, and nonlinearity coefficients. This would turn the current literature-anchored framework into a truly measured-device prediction tool.

The second direction is to improve robustness to hardware-instance shift. The zero-shot transfer experiments show that Tiny-ViT can overfit to a single D2D realization even when it is stable under same-instance noise. A natural remedy is **multi-instance HAT**, in which the training loop periodically resamples D2D masks rather than keeping a single fixed instance for the entire run. Such a strategy would directly target the failure mode exposed in Section 5.4 and is likely more valuable than simply increasing nominal noise magnitude during training.

Third, the peripheral stack should become more adaptive rather than uniformly conservative. The ADC sweep indicates that a global 6-bit converter is near the critical threshold for transformer deployment, but the layer-sensitivity results also suggest that not all analog sub-operators deserve the same precision budget. A mixed-precision peripheral design, where the most fragile projections receive higher ADC precision and more robust layers are evaluated with cheaper converters, could improve the energy-accuracy Pareto frontier without requiring better materials alone.

Finally, the present work should be extended beyond first-order behavioral assumptions in two complementary ways. On the device side, future profiles should incorporate measured conductance INL tables, state-dependent retention drift, and more pulse-faithful write dynamics. On the workload side, the conclusions should be validated with larger-scale or domain-specific datasets once the necessary external data and measured-device profiles are available. Together, these steps would preserve the current framework's profile-substitution structure while tightening the physical fidelity needed for device-specific prediction.

Furthermore, future validation should extend the present shared-regime AIHWKIT baseline into broader cross-benchmarking against industrial-grade simulation toolkits such as AIHWKIT and CrossSim. While the current framework is optimized for the photoresponse and profile-substitution needs of organic optoelectronic synaptic transistors, establishing numerical alignment under shared noise and quantization regimes remains important for community adoption.
